From Simulation to Verification:
The Case for Reclassifying Advanced Large Language Models
as Artificial Simulated General Intelligence
Tom Nelmes
Founder & Chief Architect, Aletheia
ABSTRACT
The  field  of  artificial  intelligence  has  produced  systems  of  genuine  and 
extraordinary capability — among the most significant technological achievements 
in human history. This paper does not dispute that. It asks a prior question that the 
dominant discourse has not addressed: on what epistemological foundation does 
the knowledge within these systems rest? We argue that advanced large language 
models  —  those  demonstrating  sustained  cross-domain  reasoning,  novel 
synthesis, and general cognitive capability across multiple domains — operate as 
Artificial  Simulated  General  Intelligence  (ASGI):  systems  that  reproduce  the 
outputs of understanding without having acquired that understanding through any 
verified,  independently  confirmable  process.  This  distinction  has  direct 
consequences  for  reliability,  security,  and  the  ultimate  ceiling  of  machine 
intelligence. We define ASGI, examine its implications, survey current paths  
toward AGI through this lens, and identify a specific gap that no current approach  
closes. We do not offer a complete solution. We offer a precise name for the 
problem.
1.  ESTABLISHING COMMON GROUND
Before philosophy can be productive, definitions must be honest. The vocabulary of artificial 
intelligence  has been stretched, marketed, and collapsed to the point where fundamental 
distinctions are lost. This paper requires a shared foundation.
Artificial Intelligence
AI, in its current practical form, describes computational systems trained on large datasets to 
perform  tasks  that  previously  required  human  cognition  —  language,  reasoning,  pattern 
recognition, prediction. These systems achieve this through statistical learning at scale. They are 
powerful. They are also something specific, and that specificity matters.

Large Language Model (LLM)
A large language model is a computational system trained on human-generated text at scale to 
predict and generate language. Through this process it develops the capacity for reasoning, 
synthesis, and cross-domain analysis that resembles general intelligence in its outputs. The term 
describes the architecture and training methodology — not the epistemological status of the 
knowledge produced. Not all LLMs are equivalent. A system that completes sentences within a 
narrow domain is accurately described as narrow AI. This paper argues that the most capable 
LLMs  —  those  demonstrating  sustained  cross-domain  reasoning  and  general  cognitive 
performance across multiple domains — have crossed a threshold where the LLM label actively 
misrepresents what the system is. A more accurate classification exists, and this paper proposes 
it.
Artificial General Intelligence
AGI describes a hypothetical system capable of performing any intellectual task a human can — 
not within a domain, but across all domains, including novel ones it was not explicitly trained for. 
The operative word is general. It implies transfer, adaptability, and a foundation of knowledge 
sufficient to support genuine novelty. AGI does not yet exist. Whether current architectures will 
produce it through scaling remains genuinely uncertain. This paper argues that the uncertainty 
runs deeper than the field has acknowledged.
Artificial Superintelligence
ASI describes a hypothetical system whose capabilities exceed human intelligence across all 
domains. It is the logical extension of AGI and remains firmly theoretical. The epistemological 
questions raised in this paper apply with compounding force to ASI: a superintelligence built on an 
unverified foundation would be a superintelligence built on sand.
The Question the Taxonomy Ignores
The standard classification — ANI, AGI, ASI — measures capability. It asks what the system can 
do and how broadly. It does not ask on what basis the system knows what it knows. This paper 
argues that second question is not secondary. It is prior.
2.  WHAT A LARGE LANGUAGE MODEL ACTUALLY IS
To make the central argument, precision about the nature of large language models is required.
The Compression Model
A large language model is a compression of human-generated text. During training, the model 
processes enormous quantities of written material and learns the statistical relationships between 
words, concepts, and ideas as they appear in that corpus. The result is not a database. It is a 
weighted map of relationships — a model of what tends to follow what, in the space of human 
expression.
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  2
An LLM does not know things the way a scientist knows things. It knows things the 
way a very well-read person knows things — through what others have written 
about them.
This is a meaningful and useful form of knowledge. The well-read person has genuine insight, 
reasons across domains, identifies connections specialists miss. But the well-read person also 
inherits  the  errors,  biases,  and  blind  spots  of  everything  they  have  read  —  and  has  no 
independent mechanism to verify most of what they know. Large language models have this 
problem at civilization scale.
The Citation Problem
Every fact, relationship, and inference an LLM possesses was inherited from human-generated 
text. The model’s entire knowledge base is, in a formal sense, second-hand — a citation of human 
knowledge, not an independent acquisition of it. In most circumstances this is not a problem. In 
some circumstances it is a profound one. The model has no internal mechanism to distinguish 
between knowledge that has been rigorously verified and knowledge that has not. It presents both 
with identical fluency and identical apparent confidence.
The Confidence Problem
An LLM cannot locate its own epistemic boundary from the inside. It can be prompted to express 
uncertainty. It cannot genuinely know when it is wrong. When it fails, it fails with the same surface 
texture as when it succeeds. This is not a fixable bug. It is a structural consequence of how these 
systems acquire what they know. A system that cannot locate its own confidence boundary cannot 
be fully trusted to know when it should not be trusted.
The Capability Threshold
Not all LLMs warrant reclassification. The ASGI classification applies specifically to systems that 
demonstrate: sustained reasoning across multiple genuinely disparate domains simultaneously; 
novel synthesis — connections between fields that the system can articulate and defend; adaptive 
responses to problem framings with no direct precedent in training; and general cognitive 
performance that is behaviorally indistinguishable from human expert reasoning across domains. 
A small number of frontier systems currently operating meet these criteria. The systems below this 
threshold remain accurately described as narrow AI or conventional LLMs. The threshold is real 
even where the boundary is fuzzy at the margins.
The Degrading Foundation
The epistemological problem compounds as AI capability increases. As generative AI produces 
more of the world’s written content, the proportion of AI-generated material in future training 
corpora increases. A model trained partially on the outputs of earlier models inherits not just 
human error but machine error — the confident hallucinations, the subtle biases, the systematic 
gaps of its predecessors. The epistemological foundation does not merely fail to improve with 
scale. It actively degrades as the provenance chain between training data and its original source 
lengthens. This is not a future concern. It is occurring now.

3.  THE SIMULATION ARGUMENT
What Simulation Means
Simulation, as used here, means the reproduction of the outputs of a process without the 
execution of the process itself. A flight simulator produces the experience of flying without leaving 
the ground. A weather model predicts atmospheric behavior without being the atmosphere. 
Simulation is useful, often preferable to the real thing for specific purposes. It is not the same as 
the thing it simulates.
Large language models simulate understanding. They produce the outputs of understanding — 
coherent responses, accurate predictions, meaningful connections — without executing the 
process by which understanding is actually acquired. That process, for any intelligence that has 
ever produced genuinely verified knowledge, requires some form of independent confirmation: 
forming expectations, encountering feedback, and updating based on the gap between what was 
predicted and what actually occurred.
The Turing Trap
The Turing test oriented the field toward behavioral performance as the measure of intelligence. 
Its limitation is what it ignores: behavioral performance and epistemological foundation are 
independent dimensions. A system can pass every behavioral test of intelligence while its 
knowledge rests on an entirely unverified foundation. The test tells us what the system produces. 
It tells us nothing about how it knows what it knows, or whether what it knows has ever been 
independently confirmed.
The Hard Ceiling
Simulated intelligence has a ceiling that additional simulation cannot raise. That ceiling is novelty. 
A system trained on the totality of human knowledge can recombine, interpolate, and extrapolate 
within that knowledge with remarkable sophistication. What it cannot do is generate knowledge 
that has no basis in its training distribution. True novelty — the discovery of something genuinely 
unknown — requires a form of independent confirmation that produces unexpected results. The 
result that contradicts the existing model. The observation that nothing in the training data could 
have predicted.
More simulation does not produce confirmed knowledge. It produces more simulation.
A Note on Apparent Novelty
Advanced ASGI systems do produce outputs that appear novel: unexpected connections, 
synthesis that no human has explicitly articulated before, insights that feel generative rather than 
reproductive. This is real and valuable. It is recombination and interpolation at extraordinary scale 
and sophistication.
It is not the same as confirmed discovery. A scientist who makes a novel discovery can trace the 
reasoning, reproduce the result, show their working, and explain what surprised them and why. An 
ASGI system producing an apparently novel output has none of those things available — not 
because the output is necessarily wrong, but because the mechanism that produced it is opaque 
even to the system itself.
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  4
The problem is not only that an ASGI system may produce outputs that are wrong. It is that an 
ASGI  system  cannot  reliably  distinguish  between  a  genuine  insight  and  a  sophisticated 
confabulation — and neither can the person relying on it, without independent confirmation. This 
opacity is the load-bearing problem. It is why the classification matters most precisely where the 
outputs appear most impressive.
4.  INTRODUCING ASGI
Why a New Term
Artificial General Intelligence carries an implicit promise: that the intelligence it describes is 
general in a meaningful sense. General implies transfer, adaptability, and a foundation sufficient 
to  support  genuine  novelty.  The  structural  difference  between  knowledge  that  has  been 
independently confirmed and knowledge that has been inherited from human text is not a 
quantitative gap. It is a categorical one. Describing frontier systems as approaching AGI implies 
the path from here is a matter of scale. This paper argues that assumption deserves challenge.
ASGI Defined
Artificial Simulated General Intelligence (ASGI) describes any system that demonstrates broad 
cognitive capability across multiple domains while acquiring its knowledge through training on 
human-generated data rather than through any independently confirmable process — and 
possessing no internal mechanism to distinguish between the two.
A small number of frontier large language models currently meet this definition. Not all LLMs do. 
The classification applies where the capability is genuinely general — sustained cross-domain 
reasoning, adaptive synthesis, expert-level performance across multiple fields simultaneously. 
This is not a failure. It is a precise description of what these systems are and, critically, what they 
are not.
ASGI is itself a spectrum. At one end sit conventional LLMs — capable, useful, accurately 
described by existing taxonomy. At the other end sit frontier systems whose general capability is 
genuine  and  whose  epistemological  limitations  are  correspondingly  consequential.  The 
reclassification applies at the frontier end of that spectrum.
The Revised Spectrum
— Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI) — capable within a specific domain, no meaningful 
transfer
— Large Language Model (LLM) — broad language capability, useful synthesis, below the 
general reasoning threshold
— Artificial Simulated General Intelligence (ASGI) — broad general capability across 
domains, knowledge rests on inherited simulation without any independently confirmable 
grounding
— Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) — broad capability, knowledge rests on a confirmed, 
independently verifiable foundation
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  5
— Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) — capability exceeding human intelligence across all 
domains
On this spectrum, the question is not only how capable is the system, but on what foundation does 
that capability rest. ASGI and AGI may be behaviorally indistinguishable in many circumstances. 
They are not epistemologically equivalent.
5.  THE EPISTEMOLOGICAL PROBLEM
This is the central section of the paper.
How Confirmed Knowledge Differs
There is a difference between knowing something because you have been told it, and knowing 
something  because  you  have  confirmed  it.  This  distinction  is  ancient  in  philosophy  and 
foundational in science. It is the difference between inherited belief and earned knowledge. The 
question this paper raises is simple: which kind does an LLM possess?
The answer is the former. Every claim an LLM makes rests on what humans have written. Not on 
what has been tested. Not on what has been confirmed. On what has been written. These overlap 
significantly. They are not identical. And at the margins — in novel domains, contested areas, and 
anywhere deliberate manipulation is possible — the gap between them is where everything 
important happens.
The Poisoning Problem
If the training corpus contains systematically false information — through honest error, ideological 
bias, or deliberate manipulation — the model will reproduce that false information with identical 
fluency and confidence as accurate information. It has no independent check. It cannot detect the 
corruption. It will defend it when challenged, apply it to new situations, and present it as 
knowledge.
You cannot poison what was independently confirmed. You can only poison what 
was told.
This is not a theoretical concern. Training data provenance is genuinely difficult to verify at the 
scale required for frontier models. As generative AI becomes more capable, the proportion of AI-
generated content in the information environment increases — content that may itself encode the 
errors of earlier models, compounding across generations without detection. Each generation of 
models trained on AI-generated content inherits and amplifies the errors of its predecessors. The 
epistemological foundation does not stabilise with scale. It compounds in its unreliability.
The Academic Alarm
The poisoning problem is no longer theoretical. It is arriving in university classrooms and 
research institutions faster than the field has been able to respond.
Academics across disciplines are sounding a genuine alarm. AI-generated papers and 
submissions are proliferating at a rate that detection tools cannot reliably match. The more 
capable the system, the more convincing the output — and the more deeply the errors within it 
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  6
are obscured by fluency. A poorly written fabrication is easy to identify. A confidently written, 
stylistically coherent paper whose citations are subtly wrong, whose methodology is plausible 
but flawed, and whose conclusions rest on inherited assumptions rather than independent 
analysis is genuinely difficult to distinguish from legitimate scholarship.
The deeper problem is not fraud. It is contamination. Academic literature is one of the primary 
sources from which future AI systems will be trained. Papers generated by ASGI systems — 
carrying their inherited errors, their undetected biases, their confabulated citations — are 
entering the corpus that will train the next generation of models. Those models will inherit the 
errors as if they were established knowledge, reproduce them with the same confident fluency, 
and pass them forward again.
This is the compounding failure the field has not yet fully reckoned with. The knowledge base 
does not merely fail to improve. It degrades. Each generation of models trained on AI-assisted 
academic output inherits a corpus that is fractionally less reliable than the one before it — and 
has no mechanism to detect the difference. The simulation becomes, incrementally, a 
simulation of a simulation. The distance from any original confirmed source grows with every 
iteration.
The professors raising the alarm are identifying a symptom. The ASGI classification names the 
disease.
Questions the Field Has Not Answered
How does a system trained entirely on human-generated text know which parts of that text are 
accurate? How does it locate the boundary between what is known and what is assumed? How 
does it detect when its simulation has quietly diverged from what is actually true? How does it 
defend itself against a training corpus that has been manipulated?
Where, precisely, is the knowledge grounded? Every current approach — however sophisticated 
— ultimately traces back to a corpus that was assembled by humans, reflects the biases and 
errors of its sources, and contains no independent mechanism for distinguishing confirmed 
knowledge from plausible-sounding inherited assumption.
These are not engineering questions waiting for better architecture. They are epistemological 
questions about the nature of the knowledge itself. The field has not answered them. In most 
cases, it has not asked them.
6.  THE CURRENT ROAD TO AGI: AN HONEST ASSESSMENT
The researchers and institutions working on the following approaches are doing serious work on 
genuinely difficult problems. This section examines each through the lens this paper has 
developed — not to diminish the work, but to ask a specific question of each: does it address the 
epistemological foundation, or does it proceed without one?
Scaling
More compute, more parameters, more data. The approach has produced extraordinary results 
and remains dominant. Its epistemological limitation is structural: more training data means more 
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  7
inherited knowledge, more citations, more simulation. A model trained on ten times the data 
inherits ten times as many unverified claims. The poisoning attack surface expands in proportion 
to the ambition. Where, in a larger corpus, is the knowledge more confirmed than in a smaller one? 
Scale does not answer that question. It makes it larger.
Multimodal and Vision Models
Training on images alongside text represents a further attempt to extend the knowledge base. The 
epistemological limitation applies equally: a photograph of a result is not the result. A model 
trained on images of experiments inherits the visual representation of those experiments — not 
the measurement, the instrument error, the unreported failed trials, or the gap between what the 
image shows and what actually occurred. The simulation expands. The confirmation question 
remains unanswered.
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
RLHF shifts the feedback source from corpus text to human evaluators. An improvement in some 
respects. Human evaluators can assess whether a response seems correct; they generally 
cannot assess whether it is correct against any independent standard. The feedback is better 
calibrated. Its ultimate source is still human judgment, subject to the same biases, errors, and 
limitations as the corpus it supplements. In contested domains, RLHF may systematically encode 
consensus over accuracy. These are not equivalent.
Video-Based World Models
Training on video rather than text represents the most serious architectural challenge to the 
LLM paradigm currently being pursued. The argument is that observation of the world — even 
recorded observation — grounds knowledge more reliably than language alone. A system that 
watches objects fall, liquids flow, and structures collapse builds internal representations of 
physical behavior that no text description fully captures. This is a genuine advance in how the 
question is being asked.
The epistemological problem migrates rather than resolves.
A system that learns abstract representations from video of the world is still learning from 
representations. The video is not the world. It is a recording of a slice of the world, selected, 
captured, and compiled by humans, carrying every limitation of its source. The model builds 
internal predictions about likely continuations — more sophisticated than token prediction, less 
noisy than pixel prediction, but still prediction from inherited patterns rather than confirmation 
from independent results.
The proponents of this approach are aware of the limitation. The co-founder of the most heavily 
funded world model company currently operating wrote in their own funding announcement that 
systems trained by self-supervised learning on observed data "mimic intelligence" rather than 
genuinely understanding the world. That sentence was written as a criticism of LLMs. It applies 
with equal precision to a system that learns by predicting abstract representations of video.
The question this paper asks — where is the knowledge confirmed, and how does the system 
distinguish a genuine causal model from a sophisticated pattern that fits its training data — is 
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  8
not answered by predicting what should come next in a video sequence. It is restated in more 
interesting language.
More sophisticated simulation does not produce confirmed knowledge. It produces more 
sophisticated simulation.
The Remaining Gap
Each approach addresses real limitations. None directly answers the epistemological foundation 
question. The field is asking how to make simulation more capable, more accurate, more aligned 
with human judgment. Those are valuable questions. But improved simulation, however far it is 
taken, does not produce confirmed knowledge. It produces better simulation.
The gap between ASGI and genuine AGI is not a capability gap. It is not a scale gap. It is a gap in 
the nature of the knowledge the system possesses — in whether that knowledge rests on 
something  that  has  been  independently  confirmed,  or  only  on  what  has  been  inherited, 
accumulated, and passed forward without any mechanism for verification.
That question — where is the knowledge actually grounded? — is the question this paper is 
asking. It has not been answered. It has barely been asked.
7.  IMPLICATIONS
The epistemological foundation of machine intelligence is not an academic question. It determines 
what can be trusted, what can be built on, and what the long-term trajectory of these systems will 
be.
Trust and Verifiability
A system whose knowledge rests on a confirmed, independently auditable foundation offers 
something qualitatively different from a system whose knowledge rests on inherited text. It knows 
where its knowledge ends. It can report its confidence boundary with genuine precision. The 
person relying on it knows what the system knows and, critically, what it does not. That is the 
foundation on which real trust can be built — not behavioral fluency, but traceable provenance.
High-Stakes Deployment
The contexts where ASGI systems are most confidently deployed are precisely the contexts 
where the confidence problem is most dangerous. A physician using AI for diagnosis, a lawyer 
using AI for case research, an engineer using AI for structural calculations — these applications 
involve consequential decisions where the system’s inability to locate its own epistemic boundary 
is not a theoretical concern. It is a direct patient safety, legal liability, or structural integrity issue.
This is not an argument against deployment. It is an argument for honest classification. A system 
deployed in a high-stakes context should be understood for what it is: a system of extraordinary 
capability resting on a knowledge foundation that is inherited, unconfirmed, and opaque about its 
own limits. That understanding should inform how it is used, what decisions it is permitted to make 
alone, and what independent verification it requires before its outputs are acted upon.
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  9
Alignment and the Unconfirmed Foundation
AI alignment research attempts to ensure that advanced AI systems pursue goals that are 
beneficial to humanity. Much of this work assumes the knowledge base of the system is 
essentially sound — that the problem is ensuring the system pursues the right objectives with the 
knowledge it has. The ASGI classification raises a prior question: what if the knowledge base itself 
is systematically unreliable in ways the system cannot detect?
A system optimizing powerfully toward a goal based on knowledge that has been subtly corrupted 
— through training data manipulation, through the compounding errors of AI-generated training 
content, through the systematic biases of the corpus it inherited — is a different alignment problem 
than the field has primarily addressed. Confirmed knowledge is not merely a reliability question. It 
is a prerequisite for meaningful alignment.
Scientific Discovery
AI systems applied to scientific hypothesis generation and experimental design inherit the errors, 
biases, and blind spots of the literature on which they were trained. Replication crises, publication 
bias, and the systematic under representation of null results mean that the training corpus for 
scientific AI is not a neutral representation of what is known. A system that could distinguish 
between claims inherited from a literature it cannot audit and claims that have been independently 
confirmed would represent something genuinely new. Not better simulation of science. A different 
epistemological category entirely.
Equality of Access to Truth
The most powerful AI systems are controlled by a small number of organizations that choose what 
is included in training. This is not necessarily sinister — curation at scale is genuinely difficult. But 
it means the epistemological foundation of the most powerful systems is shaped by choices that 
are not transparent and not easily audited by those who rely on them. A knowledge architecture in 
which the basis for claims can be independently examined and confirmed would distribute 
epistemic authority differently. The ground truth would not be whoever assembled the corpus.
The Stakes
These systems are being deployed in hospitals, courtrooms, laboratories, and institutions that 
affect the lives of billions of people. The epistemological foundation of those systems is not an 
abstraction. Getting it right matters. Naming it correctly is where getting it right begins.
8.  A NOTE ON NAMING
ASGI is not offered as a criticism of the work that produced current AI systems. It is offered as an 
honest label for what that work has produced — so that the distance from here to genuine general 
intelligence can be measured accurately, and so that the work required to close that distance can 
be identified clearly.
The systems described in this paper as ASGI are extraordinary achievements. They deserve a 
name that is accurate. An accurate name tells us both what they are and what they are not.
© 2026 Tom Nelmes / Aletheia  ·  10
What they are not is a system whose knowledge has been independently confirmed. No current 
system is. No current road map has a clear answer for how to build one. The field has asked how 
to make simulation more capable. It has not yet seriously asked how to confirm what the 
simulation knows.
The intelligence we have built is simulated. The intelligence we need is confirmed. 
These are not the same thing, and the difference between them is not a matter of  
scale.
That  question  —  how  does  a  machine  intelligence  acquire  knowledge  that  has  been 
independently confirmed, and how does it know the difference — is the question this paper is 
asking. It has not been answered. It has barely been asked.
The systems this paper reclassifies as ASGI are not diminished by the label. They are accurately 
described by it. An honest name for what a thing is creates the conditions for building what it is not 
yet. That is the only purpose of this paper.
At  Aletheia  we  are  working  on  ASI  alignment,  philosophy,  epistemology,  verification,  and 
engineering angles completely different to the current status quo across the board — while using 
the astronomical achievements of the people making these phenomenal ASGI platforms for the 
future of ASI and the betterment of humanity.

KEY DEFINITIONS
Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)
AI capable within a specific domain, no meaningful transfer to other domains.
Large Language Model (LLM)
A computational system trained on human-generated text at scale. Capable of sophisticated language tasks and useful 
synthesis. Below the threshold of general cross-domain cognitive capability that warrants ASGI reclassification.
Artificial Simulated General Intelligence (ASGI)
AI demonstrating broad general cognitive capability across multiple domains whose knowledge rests on inherited 
simulation rather than any independently confirmable process — and possessing no internal mechanism to distinguish 
between the two. A small number of frontier large language models currently meet this definition.
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
Hypothetical AI with broad capability whose knowledge rests on a confirmed, independently verifiable foundation.
Artificial Superintelligence (ASI)
Hypothetical AI exceeding human capability across all domains.
The Citation Problem
The structural condition whereby an LLM’s knowledge is entirely inherited from human-generated text and therefore 
only as reliable as the least trustworthy step in its provenance chain.
The Poisoning Attack Surface
The vulnerability of text-trained systems to deliberate or accidental corruption of their knowledge base through 
manipulation of training sources — compounding across generations as AI-generated content enters future training 
corpora.
The Capability Threshold
The  point  at  which  an  LLM’s  demonstrated  general  cognitive  capability  across  multiple  domains  warrants 
reclassification as ASGI. Defined by sustained cross-domain reasoning, novel synthesis, adaptive response to 
genuinely novel problem framings, and expert-level performance across multiple fields simultaneously.
Simulated Knowledge
Knowledge acquired through training on human-generated representations, inherited rather than independently 
confirmed.
Confirmed Knowledge
Knowledge whose basis can be independently examined, reproduced, and audited — where the confidence it carries 
reflects something more than the frequency with which it appeared in a training corpus.
