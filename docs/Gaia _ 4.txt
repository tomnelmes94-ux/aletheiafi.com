Section 1: What a City Actually Is 
A city is not its buildings. It is not its roads or its railways or its parks or its institutions. A city is 
the accumulated daily experience of the people who live in it — the walk to the market in the 
morning, the school run, the journey to work, the evening in the square, the view from the 
window before sleep. 
Every design decision in Gaia is made in service of that experience. The plot size. The house 
footprint. The tree on the street. The tram stop three minutes from the front door. The 
roundabout that slows a car and reveals a garden. The street name in Arabic that roots the city 
in the land it was built on. The wall of a freight terminal covered in a mural so that the person 
walking past encounters art rather than logistics. 
The numbers matter — and this paper contains them. But the numbers are in service of 
something simpler. A city where the daily experience of living is genuinely good. Where the 
streets are beautiful. Where the temperature is manageable. Where everything needed is close. 
Where the night sky is visible. Where the child walking to school passes fountains and date 
palms and a roundabout planted with flowers rather than a concrete intersection managed by a 
traffic light. 
That is the design specification. Everything that follows is an attempt to deliver it. 
 
Section 2: The Land — How 50,000 Square Kilometres Is Allocated 
Gaia’s 50,000 square kilometre footprint — 224 kilometres by 224 kilometres of flat sandstone 
plateau — is allocated across eight categories. The allocation reflects the priorities of the 
design: residential land leads, green space is generous, the solar farm is smaller than it might 
appear because the physics of the site make a small farm sufficient, and reserve capacity is 
held for the adaptations that a thousand-year city will inevitably require.

The residential allocation of 45 percent — 22,500 square kilometres — accommodates 13.9 
million residential plots at the standard plot size. At average household sizes reflecting African 
multigenerational family structures, the residential zone houses between 111 and 167 million 
people at full development. With guest houses on a significant proportion of plots the upper end 
of that range and beyond is achievable within the same footprint. 
 
Section 3: The Plot 
The standard residential plot in Gaia is 0.4 acres — approximately 1,619 square metres. This is 
the design standard around which the city’s residential grid is built, the number used in all 
population calculations, and the unit that defines the character of Gaia’s neighbourhoods. 
In practice, plots will vary. Engineering constraints — cul-de-sacs, corner positions, irregular 
boundaries at district edges — produce plots ranging from approximately 0.25 acres to 0.5 
acres. The standard is 0.4 acres and the average across the city will be close to that figure. It is 
the right number to quote and the right unit to design around. 
The house footprint occupies approximately 8 percent of the plot. The remaining 92 percent is 
garden, trees, paths, outdoor living, and in many cases a guest house. This proportion is 
deliberate and significant. Most residential plots in dense urban environments are dominated by 
the building. In Gaia the building is surrounded by living space on all sides — front garden, rear 
garden, side paths, the outdoor rooms that the climate and the design philosophy make 
genuinely usable for the majority of the year. 
The first rounds of construction build to the standard design described in Section 4 — the 
optimal footprint, the standard orientation, the Roman-influenced aesthetic that gives the 
founding city its coherent character. As the construction programme matures and private builder 
markets develop — the point at which a city has enough population and economic activity to 
support architectural variety — the design standard opens up. Plots can accommodate different 
footprints, different materials, different architectural traditions. The 0.4-acre standard is 
maintained. The building on it evolves. 
This is how cities actually develop. Rome’s first buildings were not the Forum or the Pantheon. 
They were the functional structures of a growing settlement. The grandeur came with time, with 
wealth, with the ambitions of successive generations. Gaia is designed to follow the same 
trajectory — coherent and beautiful in its founding phase, richly varied in its maturity. Little Italy 
in the Italian quarter. Little Cairo in the Egyptian district. A Parisian boulevard somewhere in the 
commercial zone. A building that looks like nothing else on earth in the district where the 
architect who imagined it chose to live. All of this comes. The foundation is laid first. 
 
Section 4: The House 
The standard Gaia house is 30 feet wide by 50 feet deep — 1,500 square feet of ground floor 
footprint. It is designed to feel significantly larger than its square footage suggests, to be 
affordable to build, to perform well in extreme heat, and to produce more energy than it 
consumes. 
The slanted roofline is the defining structural feature. The front interior wall stands 10 feet tall. 
The rear interior wall stands 15 feet tall. The 5-foot rise across the 30-foot depth creates a 
roofline that serves three purposes simultaneously. It generates the loft space above the rear 
section. It creates interior volume at the front of the house — the 20 by 50 foot open-plan living, 
kitchen, and dining area has a ceiling that rises from 10 feet at the front to 15 feet at the rear, 
producing the sense of spaciousness that a 1,000 square foot room with high ceilings reliably 
creates. And it provides the south-facing rear roof surface that carries the solar panels at 
optimal angle for the site’s latitude. 
The rear 10 by 50 feet of the ground floor houses four bedrooms across the back wall with a 
shared bathroom — each bedroom approximately 10 by 12 feet, tight but entirely liveable. The 
front 20 by 50 feet is the communal heart of the home — open plan, high-ceilinged, connected 
to the garden through wide openings at the front, the room where the family eats and lives and 
receives guests. 
Above the rear bedrooms, accessed by a stair from the living area, the loft runs the full 10 by 50 
feet at 7 feet of ceiling height — 500 square feet of additional space at effectively zero additional 
structural cost, the roof that was needed anyway enclosing a habitable volume rather than dead 
space. Storage. A teenager’s room. A home office. A workshop. Whatever the family makes of 
it. 
Total usable space: approximately 2,000 square feet from a 1,500 square foot footprint. A 
genuinely spacious family home at a construction cost that the Genesis bond structure makes 
accessible to founding residents regardless of prior wealth. 
The house is not prescribed in its appearance beyond the structural principle. The 30 by 50 
footprint with the slanted roofline is the skeleton. The skin — the facade materials, the window 
proportions, the entry design, the garden arrangement — can express any architectural tradition 
compatible with the climate. The founding-phase aesthetic is sandstone and Roman influence, 
as described in Section 7 of this paper. The structural logic beneath it is universal. 
The guest house. The same structural principle applied to a smaller footprint — 20 by 30 feet, 
8-foot front wall, 12-foot rear wall, the slanted roofline, the loft above the rear section. Two 
bedrooms, a small living space, its own entrance. Built on the rear section of the same plot as 
the main house, sharing the garden, accessed from the same path. The grandparents. The 
adult child. The cousin staying through the harvest season. The multigenerational household 
that is the natural family structure of the population Gaia is built for — housed without crowding 
the main structure, sharing the land, contributing to the carbon sequestration and energy 
generation of the same plot. 
The trees. Every plot has a minimum of two trees — one on each side of the house, positioned 
to shade the walls and windows from the harshest afternoon sun while leaving the rear roof 
section open to direct solar radiation for the panels. Species are selected for the specific work 
they do in this environment. Date palms provide canopy height and partial shade without the 
dense root systems that damage foundations. Neem trees grow rapidly in arid conditions, 
provide dense shade, and have documented air-cooling effects through evapotranspiration. 
Moringa trees produce nutritious leaves, flowers, and seed pods — food production embedded 
in the garden landscape rather than confined to a separate agricultural zone. 
Every tree earns a Genesis resource creation certificate — a verified, recurring payment in local 
currency for the carbon the tree sequesters, the cooling it provides, and the food it produces. 
The act of maintaining your garden is a financial return. The household that plants and tends its 
trees is contributing to the planetary asset base that Genesis Coin is backed by. Environmental 
obligation and financial incentive are the same decision. 
Solar. The rear half of the roof — the section not shaded by the trees that shade the front — 
carries approximately 20 panels at 300 watts each. 6 kilowatts of installed capacity producing 
approximately 13,440 kilowatt-hours annually at Nubian Desert irradiance. The household 
consumes approximately 3,000 to 4,000 kilowatt-hours. The surplus — 9,000 to 10,000 
kilowatt-hours — feeds back to the grid. Every house in Gaia is a net energy exporter. The 
household receives an energy credit rather than paying an energy bill. 
 
Section 5: The Infrastructure Beneath Everything 
Before a single house is built, the infrastructure corridors are installed. This is the design 
principle that eliminates from Gaia the most expensive and disruptive maintenance problem that 
every existing city on earth faces: the need to excavate roads and streets to access the utilities 
buried beneath them. 
The modular infrastructure corridor runs beneath every street, every garden path, and every 
green space in the city. A single accessible trench contains the water mains, sewage pipes, 
electrical conduits, fibre optic cables, data lines, drainage systems, and gas lines — everything 
that in a conventional city is buried separately at different depths, installed by different 
contractors at different times, and accessed by digging through whatever was built above it. 
The top of the corridor is a removable panel system. Designed to look like a raised garden bed 
from above — planted with herbs, flowers, and ground cover consistent with the street aesthetic 
— the panels are structurally secure under normal conditions and require a specific two-person 
tool plus real-time approval from the central building management system to open. The tool 
does not function without the approval. The approval is logged, timestamped, and permanently 
recorded in the city’s infrastructure audit system. Every access event — who opened which 
panel, when, with what authorisation, for what purpose, for how long — is on the record forever. 
To repair a water main in Gaia: no excavation. No road closure. No pneumatic drills. No 
three-week disruption. The approved team opens the panel, accesses the corridor, completes 
the repair, replaces the panel. Hours not weeks. The garden bed above it looks the same when 
the work is done as when it started. 
The fibre optic network runs in the same corridor. Every house, every school, every hospital, 
every commercial building has gigabit connectivity from the day it is built — not retrofitted as an 
afterthought but installed as a foundational utility before the structure above it is completed. 
Gaia is wired from the foundation. The digital infrastructure of the twenty-first century is as basic 
as the water pipe, because in the twenty-first century it is. 
 
Section 6: The Mobility Layer 
Gaia does not require a car. This is a design achievement not a lifestyle imposition. The mobility 
layer is built so that every daily need — work, school, market, clinic, park, friend — is reachable 
without one. Cars are available. Roads accommodate them. If the residents of Gaia choose to 
own cars in large numbers the road network has expansion capacity built in from the foundation. 
But the design does not assume the car and does not depend on it. 
The HSR grid. High-speed rail runs on a grid across the full 224 by 224 kilometre footprint. 
Primary HSR spines run north-south and east-west at intervals of approximately 25 to 30 
kilometres, creating a grid of major corridors. Secondary spines run between the primary lines at 
approximately 10-kilometre intervals, serving the residential districts between the major 
corridors. The grid covers the entire city. No point in the residential zone is more than 5 to 7 
kilometres from an HSR station. 
The intersections of the primary spines are the city’s major stations — civic landmarks in their 
own right, designed with the same architectural ambition as the public buildings that surround 
them. The stations are beautiful because the stations are public buildings. Every Gaia resident 
passes through them. They deserve the same care as the university, the civic hall, the health 
campus. 
The commercial and freight operations of the stations — the logistics side, the loading 
infrastructure, the operational back-of-house — sit behind the public face. Beautiful walls. 
Murals by Gaia artists. The operational reality is present but the first thing the person walking 
past encounters is not a loading dock. It is a work of art. 
The HSR runs underground through the city’s civic core — the central district where Hurriya 
Avenue, the major civic buildings, and the primary Speech Squares are located. Underground 
through the centre means the civic heart of Gaia is genuinely car-free and rail-free at surface 
level. A walkable, human-scale public space at the core of the largest city ever built. 
The airport — on the far eastern perimeter, described in Section 9 — has a dedicated HSR 
express connection to the civic core. Arrival in Gaia from anywhere on earth means a 20 to 30 
minute train journey from the gate to the centre of the city. 
Trams. The tram network fills the residential spaces between HSR spines. Quiet, ground-level, 
woven through the streets rather than running above or below them, the trams connect 
neighbourhoods to their nearest HSR station and to each other. The typical Gaia resident’s daily 
journey is: walk to the tram stop three to five minutes from the front door. Tram to the local 
district centre, the school, the market, the clinic. On days requiring cross-city travel: tram to the 
nearest HSR station, HSR to destination. The car is never needed. For most people on most 
days the tram is not needed either — the neighbourhood is walkable. 
Bicycles and scooters. The street network is designed for cyclists and scooter riders as primary 
users rather than afterthought accommodations. Dedicated lanes, separated from vehicle traffic, 
running through every residential district and connecting to the tram and HSR network at every 
station. The flat topography of the sandstone plateau — no hills, no gradients — makes cycling 
genuinely practical for the full population across the full city in a way that hilly terrain prevents. 
Waterways. The canal network that runs through Gaia — the thermal regulation infrastructure 
described in Paper 2 — is also a transport network. Small electric watercraft, gondola-style 
vessels, and water taxis move people and goods along the canals at low speed. Freight 
deliveries to waterfront commercial properties. Leisure travel through the park system. A 
dimension of the city’s transport network that is also one of its most beautiful features. 
Roads and roundabouts. Roads exist throughout Gaia. They are designed for the full range of 
private vehicles — if Gaia’s residents choose to own cars at Western rates the roads 
accommodate them. The expansion capacity is built in. But the default intersection management 
is a roundabout, not a traffic light. Roundabouts with generous planting at their centres — native 
desert species, flowering plants, date palms where scale allows — turn every intersection into a 
small landscape feature rather than a safety device. The city moves more slowly at its 
intersections than a traffic-light city does. That is not a flaw. A city designed for human 
experience rather than vehicle throughput should move at a pace that allows it to be 
experienced. 
Freight. All freight movement within Gaia is by rail. No large trucks on residential streets. The 
maintenance implications of heavy freight vehicles on urban roads — the surface damage, the 
noise, the safety risk to cyclists and pedestrians — are entirely avoidable and are avoided. 
Freight enters the city by the HSR freight network, terminates at distributed depot nodes at HSR 
stations throughout the grid, and travels the last kilometre by smaller electric vehicles, 
waterways, and drones. The depot is the busy, industrial part. The residential street is quiet. 
The drone delivery network — the Amazon of the water, as conceived in the planning 
documents that precede this paper — handles individual parcel delivery. Short distances, small 
vehicles, no road congestion. The depot’s proximity to every residential area means drone flight 
times are measured in minutes. The package arrives at the garden gate. 
 
Section 7: The Streets — Names, Materials, Aesthetic 
The streets of Gaia are named in Arabic. The language of the region, the language of the 
majority of the founding population, the language in which the city’s civic life will be conducted. 
English translations appear in smaller text beneath — a gesture of legibility for the global city 
Gaia will become. The primary language is the land’s language. 
The five primary civic spines carry names that describe their function and their character. 
Hurriya Avenue — حرية — Freedom. The civic spine running north-south through the city centre, 
underground HSR below, surface level pedestrian and tram, city hall at its heart. Wide enough 
for processions and celebrations. Lined with date palms and public fountains. The axis around 
which Gaia’s public life organises itself. 
Elm Avenue — تعليم — Education. The knowledge corridor running east-west through the 
university district, the school zones, the libraries, the research institutions. The street that a child 
walks to school on and a PhD student cycles to the laboratory on. 
Adalah Lane — عدالة — Justice. The legal and civic services corridor. Courts, legal offices, public 
administration, the police headquarters. Not hidden in a government quarter but present in the 
city’s daily fabric — the institutions of justice visible and accessible rather than remote. 
Sihha Boulevard — صحة — Health. The health corridor connecting the Global Health Campus to 
the network of neighbourhood clinics and hospitals that serve Gaia’s residents. The street that 
connects the world’s most ambitious healthcare infrastructure to the daily life of the people it 
exists to serve. 
Tijara Street — تجارة — Commerce. The market and commercial district. The street where 
business happens, where the city’s economic life is most concentrated and most visible. 
The numbered residential grid — north-south and east-west streets within each residential 
quarter — carries names from Nubian and African history in Arabic, with English translation 
beneath. The city’s streets are a continuous act of cultural acknowledgment. The people whose 
land this was, whose continent this is, whose history runs through this plateau for thousands of 
years before Gaia’s first foundation is poured — their names are on the streets. 
The aesthetic of the founding city is ancient Rome interpreted through the materials and climate 
of the Nubian Desert. Sandstone facades — the plateau’s own material, quarried locally, 
thermally stable, beautiful in the quality of light it produces in strong sun. Colonnaded public 
buildings providing shade at ground level while maintaining visual connection to the street — the 
architecture of the Mediterranean and the Near East, appropriate to this climate, appropriate to 
this latitude, appropriate to the permanence that a city designed to last a thousand years should 
express. Public fountains at intersections and in squares — water made visible and beautiful, 
the thermal regulation infrastructure wearing its civic face. Date palms on every primary street, 
their scale appropriate to the wide avenues of a city designed for people rather than cars. 
Over time — as the founding city establishes its character and private development markets 
mature — Gaia’s architecture diversifies. Little Italy in the district where Italian architects and 
their families built homes. Little Cairo in the neighbourhood where the Egyptian construction 
workforce put down roots and built something that reminded them of home. A Parisian 
boulevard in the commercial quarter. A building unlike anything else on earth in the district 
where the architect who imagined it chose to live. This is how great cities actually develop. The 
founding aesthetic provides the coherent foundation. The generations that follow provide the 
variation that makes a city endlessly interesting. Rome did not look the same in AD 100 as it did 
in AD 1000. Neither will Gaia. 
 
Section 8: The Parks — Where 7,500 Square Kilometres Becomes Incomprehensible 
The parks, waterways, and green corridors allocation — 7,500 square kilometres — is genuinely 
difficult to comprehend at human scale. Walt Disney World in its entirety, all 111 square 
kilometres, fits inside this allocation 67 times. The entire Greater London area — 1,572 square 
kilometres — fits inside it nearly five times. The parks of Gaia are not a feature of the city. They 
are a dimension of it. 
Within the 7,500 square kilometres: 
Disney Africa. The full Walt Disney World equivalent — all four theme parks, all the hotels, the 
water parks, the entertainment district, the transport infrastructure. Approximately 111 square 
kilometres. One point five percent of the parks allocation. Encircled by a vast park — planted, 
maintained, inhabited by wildlife — that provides the buffer between the entertainment complex 
and the residential city. Roads pass through the encircling park gently, lined with trees. Human 
bridges — elevated green crossings planted with native vegetation — carry pedestrians, 
cyclists, and wildlife over those roads rather than under them. Animals use them naturally when 
they are designed correctly. The person walking from the residential district to Disney Africa 
crosses the park road on a planted bridge without noticing the traffic below. 
The zoo and animal sanctuary. Adjacent to Disney Africa, connected by the encircling park. Not 
a conventional zoo — a rescue and sanctuary operation at the scale that a city of this ambition 
can support. Animals that cannot survive in the wild. Wildlife rescued from across the continent. 
Educational. Emotional. The place where a child in Gaia grows up understanding what lives in 
Africa alongside them. 
The freshwater lake. A freshwater lake in the Nubian Desert, fed from the aquifer and 
maintained by the city’s water recycling architecture — the same water, accounted for in the 
same ledger, not depleted but held in a different form. Swimmable. Navigable by small 
watercraft. Home to birdlife. A landscape feature that no desert city has ever had and that 
changes the character of the surrounding districts in ways that no other single design decision 
can match. The aquifer’s water held above ground, visible, beautiful, and ecologically managed 
rather than sealed beneath the sandstone. 
Stadiums. Multiple. Football. Athletics. Cricket. The Africa Kings stadium — the largest on the 
continent, the largest in this programme, a civic landmark visible from significant distances 
across the flat plateau. Large enough to host the African Cup of Nations, the Athletics World 
Championships, the Cricket World Cup, and in time the Olympic Games. The stadium is not just 
a sports venue. It is the place where a hundred million people’s stories intersect. It is where 
Gaia becomes a feeling rather than a plan. 
Museums, aquariums, cultural institutions. The aquarium fed by the Red Sea connection — 
marine life from one of the world’s most biodiverse coral ecosystems, displayed and researched 
200 kilometres from its source. Museums of African history, natural history, science, art. The 
cultural infrastructure that a city of this ambition inherits from its founding generation and adds 
to across every generation that follows. 
Speech Squares. Multiple locations distributed across every district of the city. Purpose-built 
civic assembly spaces — large enough for tens of thousands of people, with water, shade, 
sanitation, and acoustic design for outdoor gatherings. Not blocking roads. Not blocking rail. 
Designed for every type of gathering: celebrations, elections, religious observance, protest, 
markets, concerts, vigils, festivals. The constitutional right to assemble is built into the physical 
infrastructure of the city. Every district has a space designed for exactly that purpose. No one 
needs to block a road to be heard. 
The bat habitats and wildlife corridors. Designed into the park system and green corridors from 
the foundation. Bat roosts integrated into park structures and bridge undercrofts — a single bat 
consumes approximately 1,000 mosquitoes per hour, providing natural insect control at scale 
without chemical intervention. Purple martin and swallow habitats in the waterway system. 
Dragonfly populations in the water gardens, addressing mosquito larvae at the source. The city 
manages its insect ecology through the wildlife it deliberately attracts rather than through 
chemical intervention. 
 
Section 9: The Institutions 
The University of Gaia. The largest single university campus by student enrollment in human 
history. The current record is approximately 72,000 students on a single campus — Texas A&M 
at College Station. The University of Gaia targets ten times that figure at full development: 
700,000 students on campus. The long-term ambition, as the city grows and the university 
grows with it, is to exceed one million students on a single campus — a scale that has never 
existed and that Gaia is the only city on earth with the land and the intent to achieve. 
Admission is free. For anyone. From anywhere on earth. Entry is determined by academic merit 
— the best students regardless of nationality, background, or economic position. First come first 
served until capacity is reached. When oversubscribed the tiebreaker is financial need. The 
student from a poor family in Sudan gets the place over an equally qualified student from a 
wealthy family elsewhere — not because merit does not matter but because merit got them both 
there and the differentiator should favour the person for whom this opportunity changes 
everything rather than the person for whom it is one of many options. 
The campus is a city within the city. Residential halls, research facilities, sports infrastructure, 
cultural spaces, its own internal tram network. Connected to the city’s HSR grid. The University 
of Gaia is the single largest concentration of human intellectual capital assembled in one place 
on earth — by design, from the foundation, permanently. 
The Global Health Campus. The largest health campus ever constructed. World-class IVF at 
cost — the treatment that changes families’ lives, available at what it actually costs to provide 
rather than what a market charges. Free serious treatments: complex surgery, specialist 
oncology, rare disease treatment. The category of intervention that people currently travel 
internationally for because it is unavailable or unaffordable at home — available here, free, to 
anyone who can reach Gaia. Genesis contracts with global airlines to establish subsidised 
medical travel routes. The airline gets a route to the world’s fastest-growing city. The patient 
gets there. 
The world-class NICU — built in partnership with the leading neonatal programmes globally, 
staffed through the University of Gaia’s medical faculty, operating at the standard of the best 
hospitals on earth. Africa has the highest neonatal mortality rates of any continent. Gaia builds 
the NICU capacity the continent has never had — not as a temporary programme but as 
permanent infrastructure, growing with the city, improving continuously. The baby who would 
have died in a regional hospital elsewhere lives in Gaia. That is the metric. That is what success 
looks like. 
The Global Health Campus serves the world. The network of subsidiary hospitals and clinics 
distributed throughout Gaia’s residential districts serves the city’s residents. Every district has a 
clinic. Every major residential zone has a hospital. The Global Health Campus is the pinnacle. 
The local clinic is where most people’s healthcare actually happens. Both are funded by Gaia’s 
export revenues and the city’s tax base. 
The Genesis Clearing House and non-profit headquarters. The financial hub of the world’s 
monetary architecture, located in the largest city on the most important continent for the system 
it serves. Thousands of economists, accountants, diplomats, and financial engineers. 
High-paying, intellectually demanding roles that anchor Gaia’s professional class from its 
earliest years. The organisation that is building the world is headquartered in the city it built first. 
The Africa Kings and the stadium. The largest stadium on the continent in the largest city ever 
built. The Africa Kings — Gaia’s NFL franchise — playing in a venue that also hosts the African 
Cup of Nations, the Athletics World Championships, the Cricket World Cup, and in time the 
Olympic Games. Sport creates identity. Identity creates belonging. Belonging creates 
permanence. The Africa Kings playing in Gaia creates a reason for people across the continent 
and across the world to follow what is happening here. Every broadcast is an argument for what 
Gaia is becoming. 
The airport. Located on the far eastern perimeter — the side facing the Red Sea corridor and 
the Cairo-Cape Town transport spine. The largest airport ever constructed by design and 
ambition. Buffered from the residential city by Disney Africa and its encircling park. Adjacent to 
the city’s largest commercial zone, its largest freight rail depot, and the largest concentration of 
logistics infrastructure. Connected to the civic core by the dedicated HSR express line — 20 to 
30 minutes from gate to centre. 
The airport’s commercial geography is genuinely extraordinary. Within a four-hour flight radius: 
Cairo, Addis Ababa, Nairobi, Khartoum, Riyadh, Dubai, Mumbai. Within an eight-hour radius: 
most of Europe, all of the Middle East, South and Southeast Asia, the eastern seaboard of 
Africa. Gaia’s airport is not remote. It is central to the most important air routes connecting three 
continents. 
 
Section 10: Light, Night, and Stars 
A design paper for Gaia would be incomplete without addressing what is absent as well as what 
is present. 
Gaia has minimal light pollution. Not no lighting — the city is safe, functional, and beautiful at 
night. But the lighting is directional, downward-facing, warm-spectrum, designed for the human 
eye at human scale rather than for visibility from satellites. Street lights illuminate the pavement 
and the faces of the people walking on it. They do not illuminate the sky above. 
The consequence is that the night sky above Gaia is visible. The Milky Way is visible. The stars 
that the Nubian Desert has always shown to the people who lived in it — and that every city built 
with the conventional approach to outdoor lighting has taken away from the people who live in it 
— are visible from every residential plot, every park, every street corner in Gaia. 
This is not a minor aesthetic choice. It is a statement about what a city owes the people who live 
in it. The universe is there. The city that was built to complement the planet it sits on should not 
obscure it.
