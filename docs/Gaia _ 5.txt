Section 1: What Gaia Is Made Of 
Cities are made of materials. Not plans, not designs, not governance frameworks — materials. 
Steel. Cement. Sand. Glass. Copper wire. Fibre optic cable. Solar panels. HSR track sections. 
The modular components of the infrastructure corridor system. The roof tiles and sandstone 
blocks and window frames of 13.9 million homes. 
Every one of these materials must be extracted, processed, manufactured, transported to a Red 
Sea port, moved 200 kilometres overland to the construction site, and placed in its correct 
location in the correct sequence. Before a single resident moves in. Before the university opens. 
Before the Africa Kings play their first game. 
This paper examines what Gaia requires, where it comes from, how it arrives, and how the 
phasing of that supply chain protects the city’s development from the disruptions that 
unconstrained demand would produce. It also acknowledges, with the honesty that 
characterises this series throughout, that the global material supply chains Gaia draws on are 
themselves under structural pressure — not primarily from Gaia, but from the simultaneous 
convergence of every major technology transition of the current era onto the same materials at 
the same time. 
The Genesis programme is acutely aware of this planetary-scale materials challenge. The 
companion white papers to Paper 8 of the Genesis Civilisation Papers address it in full — the 
seabed research programme, the rare earth processing infrastructure, the substitution 
technology investments, the sodium-ion battery development, the synthetic fuels programme. 
That is the global response to a global problem. 
For Gaia specifically, the picture is more manageable than the global headlines suggest. The 
city’s primary materials — steel, cement, sand, aggregate, sandstone, glass — are not exotic 
minerals facing supply crises. They are the oldest and most abundant construction materials in 
human history. The materials that are genuinely constrained — copper, silver, the rare earth 
elements in the HSR electrification systems — are required at volumes that careful phased 
procurement can address without competing destructively with global supply chains. The 
sequencing described in this paper is itself a materials management strategy: building what 
requires abundant materials first, phasing the procurement of constrained materials across the 
decades of the construction programme. 
 
Section 2: The Bulk Materials — Steel, Cement, Sand, Stone 
These are the materials that will be required in the largest volumes. They are not scarce. They 
are heavy, expensive to move, and energy-intensive to produce. The logistics of getting them to 
the Nubian plateau — rather than their availability — is the primary challenge. 
Steel. 
The standard Gaia house uses approximately 6 tonnes of steel in its reinforced concrete 
structure — rebars, structural columns, floor slabs, roof supports. This figure is derived from 
standard construction industry data: approximately 4 kilograms of reinforcement steel per 
square foot of built-up area for low-rise construction, applied to a 1,500 square foot footprint. 
For one million houses — approximately the founding phase target — steel demand is 
approximately 6 million tonnes. For the full 13.9 million plots at standard construction that is 
approximately 83 million tonnes of structural steel for residential construction alone. 
These are large numbers in isolation. In context they are manageable. China’s annual steel 
production is approximately 1 billion tonnes. Global annual steel production is approximately 1.9 
billion tonnes. The residential steel requirement for Gaia’s full build-out — 83 million tonnes — 
represents approximately 4 percent of one year’s global steel output, spread across decades of 
phased construction. The founding phase of one million homes requires approximately 6 million 
tonnes — less than one percent of annual global production. 
The HSR grid adds a separate and significant steel demand. China’s 50,000 kilometre network 
consumed approximately 133 million tonnes of steel across its full buildout — approximately 
2,660 tonnes per kilometre of track. Gaia’s HSR grid spans approximately 2,000 kilometres of 
primary and secondary spines across the 224 by 224 kilometre footprint. At Chinese material 
intensity: approximately 5.3 million tonnes of structural and rail steel for the HSR system. 
Total steel across the full Gaia programme — residential, HSR, civic buildings, the port, the 
infrastructure corridors, the solar farm structures, the utility systems — is in the range of 100 to 
120 million tonnes across the full development timeline. This is a significant quantity. It is not an 
extraordinary one for a programme of this scale and duration. China built its railway 
infrastructure and simultaneously its cities on volumes of this order over comparable timescales. 
Cement. 
The standard house uses approximately 14 tonnes of cement — derived from the standard 
figure of approximately 0.4 cement bags of 50 kilograms per square foot of construction, applied 
to the 1,500 square foot footprint. 
For one million houses: approximately 14 million tonnes of cement. For the full 13.9 million 
plots: approximately 195 million tonnes across the full residential programme. 
Combined with the infrastructure corridors, the HSR foundations and viaducts, the road base, 
the civic buildings, and the port expansion, total cement demand across the full programme is in 
the range of 300 to 400 million tonnes. China produces approximately 2.3 billion tonnes of 
cement annually. The global industry produces approximately 4.4 billion tonnes. Gaia’s cement 
demand, spread across decades, is a fraction of a single year’s global output. 
The constraint is not global supply. It is logistics — cement is heavy and expensive to ship long 
distances. The solution is local production. A cement plant on or near the Gaia site — drawing 
on the local limestone and sandstone that the Nubian plateau provides in abundance, powered 
by the solar energy that the site generates in surplus — is among the early Phase Zero 
investments. Local cement production eliminates the shipping cost, reduces the construction 
timeline dependency on external supply chains, and contributes directly to Sudan’s industrial 
development as a permanent productive asset beyond the construction phase. 
Sand and aggregate. 
The construction of Gaia requires extraordinary volumes of sand and aggregate — the bulk 
materials of concrete and road base. Per house: approximately 250 cubic feet of sand and 190 
cubic feet of coarse aggregate. For one million homes: approximately 250 million cubic feet of 
each — roughly 7 million cubic metres of sand and 5.4 million cubic metres of aggregate for 
residential construction alone. The HSR viaducts and foundations consume comparable 
volumes in a different form. 
The Nubian Desert is a sandstone plateau. The raw material for both aggregate and a 
significant fraction of the sand requirement exists beneath the construction site. Crushing and 
processing operations established during Phase Zero convert local geology into 
construction-grade materials. The sandstone that is excavated to install the infrastructure 
corridors becomes the aggregate that fills those corridors’ concrete structure. The material 
extracted to create the city’s foundations is the material that builds it. Waste is not a category in 
the Gaia construction programme. It is a resource waiting to be processed. 
Sandstone. 
The aesthetic choice to build Gaia’s facades and civic structures in sandstone — the ancient 
Rome-influenced character described in Paper 4 — is also a logistical virtue. The construction 
material for the city’s public face is under the city’s feet. Local quarrying, local cutting, local 
finishing. No shipping, no import cost, no supply chain vulnerability. The warm-toned sandstone 
facades of Gaia’s founding districts are made from the Nubian plateau itself. The city is built 
from what it stands on. 
 
Section 3: The Solar Farm — Panels, Silver, and the Phasing Strategy 
The 500 square kilometre central solar farm is among the first major construction deployments 
in Gaia’s programme. Without power, nothing else functions at scale. The farm is Phase Zero 
infrastructure alongside the port, the freight corridor, and the water supply. 
What 500 km² of solar requires. 
At a modern utility-scale panel efficiency of approximately 200 watts per square metre, 500 
square kilometres of installed capacity produces approximately 100 gigawatts of installed peak 
capacity — which at the Nubian Desert’s 25 percent average capacity factor produces 
approximately 25 gigawatts of average continuous output. 
The primary materials in a crystalline silicon solar panel are glass, aluminium framing, silicon 
cells, polymer encapsulants, and copper wiring for the electrical connections. None of these are 
scarce in the quantities required for 500 square kilometres of installation. 
The exception is silver. Silver is used in the conductive paste that makes the electrical contacts 
on each silicon cell — approximately 15 to 20 milligrams per watt of panel capacity. At 100 
gigawatts of installed capacity, the silver requirement is approximately 1,500 to 2,000 tonnes. 
Global annual silver production is approximately 25,000 tonnes. The Gaia solar farm’s total 
silver requirement — spread across 10 to 15 years of phased installation — represents 
approximately 6 to 8 percent of one year’s global silver output per year of installation. This is 
significant but manageable. It is not the largest single draw on global silver supply — the solar 
industry globally is already consuming approximately 20 to 25 percent of annual silver 
production. Genesis procurement for the Gaia farm enters this market as a large but not 
extraordinary buyer, with the advantage of long-term offtake contracts that provide price 
certainty and supply security rather than competing in spot markets. 
The rooftop solar on 13.9 million residential plots — approximately 20 panels per house at 300 
watts each — adds a further silver requirement of comparable magnitude, phased across 
decades as the city’s residential zones are built. The combined solar programme’s silver 
procurement is the largest single constrained-material challenge of the Gaia build. It is 
addressed through a combination of phased installation, long-term supply contracts with 
silver-producing nations — Peru, Mexico, China, Russia are the largest producers — and the 
expectation that panel technology will continue reducing silver content per watt as perovskite 
and silver-free contact technologies mature. 
China’s manufacturing dominance and what it means for Gaia. 
China manufactures approximately 80 to 85 percent of the world’s solar panels. At the cost 
levels China produces them — approximately 50 percent cheaper than European equivalents, 
65 percent cheaper than American production — the Gaia solar programme is economically 
viable at the scale required. The same Chinese construction partnership that builds the HSR 
grid and the residential districts builds the solar farm. The supply chain for panels, inverters, 
mounting structures, and grid connection equipment is Chinese, arriving at the Red Sea port on 
the same logistics infrastructure that carries the construction materials. 
 
Section 4: The HSR Network — Track, Electrification, Copper 
The Gaia HSR grid spans approximately 2,000 kilometres of primary and secondary spines. At 
Chinese material intensity — approximately 2,660 tonnes of steel per kilometre and 
approximately 32 tonnes of copper per kilometre for electrification systems — the network 
requires approximately 5.3 million tonnes of structural steel and approximately 64,000 tonnes of 
copper. 
The steel is manageable at global production volumes. The copper requires more careful 
treatment. 
Copper. 
Copper runs through everything in Gaia. The HSR electrification system. The electrical wiring in 
every building. The solar panel connections. The grid infrastructure connecting generation to 
consumption. The infrastructure corridor’s electrical conduits. The fibre optic cable’s copper 
power elements. Every motor, every transformer, every switchboard. 
The global copper supply faces a projected deficit of approximately 30 percent by 2035 as 
electric vehicle demand, renewable energy buildout, grid modernisation, and AI infrastructure all 
compete for the same supply. New copper mines take 16 to 29 years from discovery to 
production in the United States. The Copperbelt — immediately adjacent to the Genesis City 3 
site in Zambia and the broader African programme — represents one of the most significant 
concentrations of copper on earth, and it is a primary target for Genesis infrastructure 
investment and supply partnership. 
For Gaia specifically, copper procurement is managed through three mechanisms. Long-term 
supply contracts with Copperbelt producers — the African programme creates a natural 
partnership between Gaia’s construction demand and the copper production capacity adjacent 
to the Genesis African city network. Efficiency in design — the infrastructure corridor system, 
which runs all electrical systems through a single accessible trench, reduces total copper 
installation length per building compared to conventional buried wiring that takes longer routes 
through less accessible pathways. And phased procurement — the 64,000 tonnes of copper for 
the HSR electrification system is procured over the 15 to 20 year construction phase rather than 
competing for global supply in a single concentrated period. 
 
Section 5: The Infrastructure Corridors — The Manufactured System 
The modular infrastructure corridor system — described in Paper 4 — is not a conventional 
construction material. It is a manufactured product: precision-engineered modular trench units, 
removable panel systems, the two-person access tools, and the central building management 
system that controls access approvals and logs every maintenance event. 
This system does not exist at commercial scale today. It exists as a design specification and an 
engineering principle. Part of the Gaia construction programme is the development and 
manufacturing scale-up of the corridor system itself — working with engineering and 
manufacturing partners to produce the components at the volumes required for 50,000 square 
kilometres of city. 
The materials in the corridor system are standard — steel, high-density polyethylene, reinforced 
concrete, the same materials used throughout the construction programme. The innovation is 
the integration and the access mechanism. The manufacturing challenge is producing uniform, 
interchangeable modular components at a scale that the construction phasing can absorb 
without bottlenecks. 
China’s prefabrication industry — which produces standardised components for HSR viaducts, 
building structures, and infrastructure systems at extraordinary scale — is the natural 
manufacturing partner for the corridor system’s components. The same factories that produce 
HSR box girders at thousands of units per month can be adapted to produce corridor modules 
at comparable volumes. The logistics are identical: manufactured at scale, shipped to the Red 
Sea port, transported to site, installed by trained crews working from standardised drawings. 
The corridor system is also where the most significant long-term savings of the Gaia programme 
accumulate. Every conventional city spends between 2 and 5 percent of its infrastructure 
replacement value annually on maintenance and repair — much of it on the excavation and 
reinstatement costs of accessing buried utilities. Gaia’s corridors eliminate this cost entirely. The 
infrastructure is accessible without excavation from the first day of the city’s operation through 
the last. The initial manufacturing and installation cost of the corridor system is repaid many 
times over across the first century of the city’s life. 
 
Section 6: Fibre Optic and Digital Infrastructure 
Every building in Gaia has gigabit fibre optic connectivity from the day it is built. The fibre runs in 
the infrastructure corridors alongside the water, power, and drainage systems. It is foundational 
utility, not optional upgrade. 
The fibre optic cable itself — glass strands carrying data as pulses of light — has no exotic 
mineral requirements. The primary material is silica glass, which is silicon dioxide: sand. 
Available in unlimited quantities. The manufacturing challenge is precision, not supply. The 
cable sheathing uses polymer materials. The connectors use small quantities of copper and 
gold. None of these are supply-constrained at the volumes Gaia’s installation requires. 
The Starlink and satellite internet network provides the construction phase connectivity — from 
day one of Phase Zero before any fibre is installed — and serves as permanent backup and 
rural coverage for the broader Nubian Corridor region beyond Gaia’s footprint. Two systems. 
Complete coverage. The physical fibre is the primary high-capacity network. The satellite is the 
resilient backup. 
No data centres inside the city. The underwater facilities at the Red Sea coast — housed in 
sealed pods at depth, cooled passively by seawater, powered by the coastal solar infrastructure 
— serve Gaia’s computational needs without generating heat or noise within the residential 
zones. The data infrastructure that powers the city is 200 kilometres away and invisible. 
 
Section 7: The Port — Gateway to the Construction Programme 
Everything Gaia requires that cannot be produced locally arrives at the Red Sea coast. The port 
is therefore not a convenience of the construction programme. It is a precondition of it. 
The existing Port Sudan facility — the primary Sudanese Red Sea port — has existing 
deep-water capacity and a track record of handling commercial freight. It requires significant 
expansion to handle Gaia construction volumes at peak. A city of this scale, built at Chinese 
construction speeds, will require port throughput at peak comparable to a mid-sized national 
economy’s entire import volume. 
The port expansion is itself a major Genesis infrastructure investment, designed in two phases. 
Phase Zero: rapid capacity expansion of the existing Port Sudan facility — additional berths, 
expanded storage, upgraded crane and handling equipment — sufficient to support the founding 
construction phase. Phase One: a new purpose-built deep-water facility at an optimal coastal 
location for the Gaia-Red Sea corridor, designed for the full construction and operational 
volumes across the city’s development lifetime. 
The port investment is not purely a construction cost. The Red Sea coast port serving Gaia is a 
permanent commercial infrastructure asset — a gateway for the import and export commerce of 
a city of 100 million people, a regional logistics hub for northeastern Africa, a facility whose 
commercial revenues accrue to the host nation as part of the 5 to 10 percent production return 
that Genesis delivers without strings attached. 
The 200-kilometre freight corridor. 
From the port to the construction site, a managed logistics road carries all materials during the 
construction phase. Designed for the axle loads of heavy freight vehicles, maintained as a 
primary infrastructure asset throughout the build programme, and eventually upgraded to a 
permanent freight rail connection when the HSR programme reaches the coastal corridor. 
The 200-kilometre distance is a logistical challenge but not an unusual one. China regularly 
operates construction logistics corridors of this length and longer in its infrastructure 
programmes in remote regions. The Qinghai-Tibet Railway’s construction required supply chains 
of comparable distances in conditions of significantly greater difficulty. The Nubian plateau is 
flat, accessible, and free of the permafrost, altitude, and terrain complexity that defined the Tibet 
programme’s logistics challenges. Two hundred kilometres of flat desert road is among the 
easier logistics problems in China’s infrastructure portfolio. 
 
Section 8: Local Production — What Gaia Makes for Itself 
A city being built from nothing in a remote location has two options for every material it requires. 
Import it or produce it locally. The economics of each depend on the material’s weight, the cost 
of shipping, and whether the raw inputs for local production exist at the site. 
For the bulk materials — cement, aggregate, processed sandstone — local production is clearly 
superior to import. The raw inputs exist under the plateau. The energy to process them is 
generated by the solar farm. The distance savings are substantial. Genesis establishes local 
production facilities for these materials as Phase Zero investments, treating them as permanent 
industrial infrastructure for Sudan rather than temporary construction plants to be 
decommissioned when the city is complete. 
For the specialised materials — solar panels, HSR track, fibre optic cable, precision electronics 
— import from Chinese manufacturers is clearly superior during the construction phase. The 
manufacturing scale and cost efficiency that Chinese production provides cannot be replicated 
locally at construction-phase volumes. Over time, as Gaia’s economy matures and its industrial 
base develops, local manufacturing of some of these products becomes viable. The city that is 
built using Chinese solar panels eventually manufactures its own. 
This transition — from imported specialised materials to locally manufactured ones — follows 
the same trajectory that Shenzhen followed. Shenzhen was built with materials and equipment 
imported from Hong Kong and beyond. Within a decade it was manufacturing those materials 
and equipment for export. The transition is not instantaneous and it is not planned in detail from 
the outset. It is the natural consequence of a city with the education, infrastructure, and 
economic activity that Gaia is designed to produce. 
 
Section 9: Phasing — The Anti-Inflation Strategy 
The most important logistics principle of the Gaia programme is that procurement is deliberately 
phased. Not because the capital is unavailable. Because deploying capital faster than the 
physical capacity to absorb it produces inflation rather than development. 
If Gaia attempts to procure all of its cement, steel, copper, and solar panels within the first five 
years, it drives up global prices for all of these materials, making its own construction more 
expensive while simultaneously damaging every other construction programme on earth that 
depends on the same supply chains. This is not the Genesis approach. Genesis buys at a scale 
and a pace that the market can supply without disruption. 
The phasing schedule — detailed in the construction management documents that accompany 
this paper — sequences procurement to match realistic supply capacity for each material 
category. Steel at volumes that represent a manageable fraction of global production in each 
year. Cement produced locally from year one. Copper procured through long-term offtake 
contracts that add to supply rather than competing for existing stock. Silver for the solar 
programme acquired across 15 years of panel installation rather than in a single purchasing 
burst. 
This phasing is also why Paper 3 of this series described the founding population as arriving 
incrementally rather than all at once. The city cannot house 100 million people before it has built 
housing for 100 million people. The housing cannot be built before the supply chain for its 
materials is established. The supply chain cannot be established before the port is expanded 
and the freight corridor is built. The sequence is the strategy. Violate the sequence and you do 
not build faster. You build more expensively and less well. 
China learned this on Shenzhen. The city did not try to be a megacity in year five. It built the 
foundations of a megacity in its first decade and let the megacity develop from those 
foundations. Gaia follows the same discipline with the same partner. 
 
Section 10: The Broader Materials Picture — Genesis Eyes Open 
This paper has focused on Gaia’s specific supply chain. The broader context deserves honest 
acknowledgment. 
The world faces a genuine critical minerals challenge. For 19 of 20 strategic minerals China is 
the leading refiner. Gallium, dysprosium, cobalt, germanium — materials essential for 
semiconductors, permanent magnets, batteries, and solar cells — face supply concentration 
risks that no amount of diplomatic goodwill fully resolves. The seabed contains vast reserves 
but extracting them at commercial scale is a decade or more away. Substitution technologies — 
sodium-ion batteries, silver-free solar contacts, synthetic alternatives to rare earth magnets — 
are advancing but are not yet at full commercial deployment. 
Genesis is not a bystander to this challenge. The programme funds seabed research. The 
programme funds substitution technology. The programme builds rare earth processing 
capability outside China through the Australia partnership and the African mineral development 
programme. These are documented in the companion white papers to Paper 8 of the Genesis 
Civilisation Papers. 
Gaia’s specific supply chain is substantially insulated from the most acute pressure points of the 
global materials challenge. The city’s primary building materials are abundant. The constrained 
materials — silver, copper, rare earth elements for HSR systems — are required at volumes that 
careful procurement management can address. The city is built in phases that allow technology 
improvements to reduce material intensity in later phases. The sodium-ion storage technology 
that may replace the molten salt thermal system in some applications uses no lithium, no cobalt, 
no rare earths. If it reaches commercial scale before Gaia’s later construction phases the 
materials picture improves further. 
The honest statement is this: Gaia is buildable with today’s materials supply chains, managed 
carefully and phased intelligently. The global materials challenge is a programme risk that 
Genesis monitors, funds research to address, and plans around — not a reason not to build.
