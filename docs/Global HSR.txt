Section 1: The Most Consequential Infrastructure Decision of the Century 
Every era of human civilisation has had a defining infrastructure. The Roman roads that made 
an empire administrable. The maritime trade routes that connected the medieval world’s 
economies. The canal networks that preceded the Industrial Revolution. The railways that made 
nation-states coherent. The highways that shaped the twentieth century’s geography of 
production and consumption. 
The defining infrastructure of the twenty-first century is high-speed rail. Not because it is the 
fastest technology available — aircraft are faster. Not because it carries the most freight — 
shipping does that. But because it is the technology that best serves the largest number of 
people across the widest range of journeys, at the lowest energy cost per passenger-kilometre, 
with the highest capacity per corridor, and with the smallest land footprint per unit of economic 
connectivity it creates. 
The world already knows this. China built 50,000 kilometres of HSR between 2008 and 2024 — 
more than the rest of the world combined — and the data on what that network produced is 
unambiguous. Cities connected to the HSR grid saw higher GDP growth, higher employment, 
higher property values, and greater economic integration with other cities on the network. The 
effect compounds with every additional connection. Every new node adds value not just to the 
new city but to every city already on the network. The network effects of rail connectivity are 
among the most powerful economic multipliers documented in modern infrastructure economics. 
What China built for itself, Genesis proposes to help build for the world. 
Not by replacing what exists. Not by competing with BRI. Not by imposing a single system on 
sovereign nations. By filling the gaps. Connecting the fragments. Building the spines that 
transform isolated segments into continental networks. And building the crossings that connect 
those continental networks to each other — creating, for the first time in human history, a single 
continuous land-based rail network spanning every inhabited continent on earth. 
This document maps that network. What exists. What is being built. What Genesis proposes to 
add. And what remains — honestly, without false modesty — a dream for the generations that 
follow. 
 
Section 2: What Already Exists — The Foundation 
The global HSR network is far more developed than most people outside the infrastructure 
community realise. The foundation for a genuinely global network is not being built from nothing. 
It is being assembled from fragments that already exist and need only to be connected. 
China — the anchor of everything. 
Fifty thousand kilometres of operational HSR as of 2025, expanding toward 60,000km by 2030 
and a planned 70,000km by 2035. The Eight Vertical Eight Horizontal grid connects every major 
Chinese city. The Beijing-Shanghai line — 1,318km — was built in three years and two months 
by 110,000 workers, opening in 2011. The Beijing-Guangzhou line — 2,298km, the world’s 
longest HSR line — connects northern and southern China end to end. Over 3 billion passenger 
journeys per year. More HSR passengers than the rest of the world combined. 
China’s network is the proof that the global vision is achievable. Everything proposed in this 
document is an extension of what China has already demonstrated is possible, in conditions 
that were in many cases more difficult than what the proposed extensions require. 
East Asia — the most connected region on earth. 
Japan’s Shinkansen — 3,500km, the original HSR network, operational since 1964 and in 60 
years of operation without a single passenger fatality. South Korea’s KTX — 1,400km. Taiwan’s 
HSR — 350km connecting the island’s west coast corridor. 
Southeast Asia is connecting progressively. Indonesia’s Jakarta-Bandung HSR — 142km — 
opened in 2023, the first operational HSR in Southeast Asia, built entirely by China’s 
construction partnership. The Laos Boten-Vientiane Railway — 414km at 160km/h — opened in 
2021, connecting Laos to China’s Yunnan province. This is the first operational section of the 
planned Kunming-Singapore corridor, one of the most strategically significant rail projects on 
earth: a continuous rail connection from Kunming in southwestern China through Laos, 
Thailand, Malaysia, and Singapore — 3,900km connecting mainland Southeast Asia to China’s 
network. 
Europe — well-connected in the west, thin in the east. 
France’s TGV — approximately 2,800km. Spain’s AVE — approximately 4,000km, the longest 
HSR network in Europe. Germany’s ICE — approximately 3,000km. Italy, Belgium, the 
Netherlands, and the UK all have operational high-speed segments connecting into the French 
network through the Channel Tunnel — the world’s most proven long sea crossing, 50km under 
the English Channel at 40m depth, operational since 1994. 
The eastern gap is significant. Romania, Bulgaria, North Macedonia, Serbia, Albania, Greece — 
limited or no HSR. The Belgrade-Budapest line, under construction with Chinese involvement, 
begins to address this. But the Balkans and southeastern Europe remain largely disconnected 
from the western European HSR network. This is where the European spine that connects to 
the Genesis African network has its most significant current gap. 
Middle East. 
Saudi Arabia’s Haramain HSR — 450km connecting Mecca, Medina, and Jeddah — is 
operational. The proposed Gulf Cooperation Council Railway, which would connect Saudi 
Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, and Oman in a continuous 2,177km network, has been 
discussed for decades but remains incomplete. The UAE sections are progressing. The full 
network is not yet built. 
Africa — the most urgent gap. 
The continent with the world’s fastest-growing population has the world’s least developed rail 
network. What exists reflects not the needs of Africa’s population but the needs of Africa’s 
colonial administrators and the extraction economy they built — lines running from 
resource-producing interior regions to coastal ports, designed to move commodities outward 
rather than to connect people and economies laterally. 
The exceptions are the Chinese-built Standard Gauge Railways of the past decade. 
Ethiopia-Djibouti: 750km electrified standard gauge at 120km/h, opened 2016. Kenya 
Mombasa-Nairobi SGR: 472km, opened 2017. Nigeria Lagos-Ibadan: 156km, opened 2021. 
Significant, functional, insufficient. Three segments that do not yet connect to each other, let 
alone to a continental network. 
The Americas — the most underserved developed economy. 
The United States, the world’s largest economy, has one operational HSR line: the Acela 
corridor connecting Boston, New York, Washington. 735km. Maximum speed 240km/h, average 
speed significantly lower due to shared track and curve constraints. It barely qualifies as HSR 
by international standards. California HSR is under construction — 119 miles of the Central 
Valley segment — with the larger system deeply contested. 
Canada, Mexico, Brazil — nothing operational. The Americas represent the largest single gap 
between economic scale and rail infrastructure on earth.

Section 4: The Genesis Difference — Same Rail, Different Finance 
The Belt and Road Initiative has built railways that Africa, Southeast Asia, and Central Asia 
genuinely needed and could not have financed themselves. The Addis Ababa-Djibouti Railway 
transformed a landlocked nation’s access to the sea. The Kenya SGR cut freight transit time 
between Mombasa and Nairobi from several days to under nine hours. These are real benefits 
to real populations. 
The financing structure behind many of these railways has produced real problems alongside 
the real benefits. Laos borrowed the equivalent of approximately 50 percent of its annual GDP 
to finance a railway through its territory. The debt is owed to Chinese state banks. The revenue 
from the railway flows partly to repay it and partly to Chinese operating partners. The net benefit 
to Laos — a genuinely landlocked nation that now has a rail connection to the world — is real. 
The debt burden that comes with it is equally real. 
Under Genesis the same railway gets built. The same Chinese engineering, the same 
construction teams, the same track and rolling stock. But the financing comes from Genesis 
bonds rather than from sovereign loans to host nations. The host nation carries no debt. It 
receives a production return from traffic revenue that grows as the network grows. The railway 
belongs to the Genesis network — governed transparently by the multilateral structure 
described in the Genesis Civilisation Papers — rather than to any single sovereign creditor. 
This distinction — identical infrastructure, fundamentally different financial architecture — 
resolves the primary political and economic objection to BRI-style infrastructure finance while 
preserving everything that makes China’s construction capability the most powerful 
infrastructure delivery mechanism in human history. 
China builds. Genesis finances. Host nations benefit without the debt that has made several 
nations’ BRI experience complicated. 
The partnership is not competitive. It is complementary. China has the construction knowledge, 
the manufacturing base, the project management systems, and the diplomatic relationships. 
Genesis has the financial architecture that makes the construction sustainable. Together they 
can build what neither can build alone — at the scale and speed that the world’s infrastructure 
deficit actually requires. 
 
Section 5: The African Network — Two Spines and the Veins Between Them 
Africa is a continent of approximately 30 million square kilometres — three times the size of 
Europe, more than three times the size of the United States. Its coastline is approximately 
30,500km. Its population will reach 2.5 billion by 2050 and 4 billion by the end of the century. 
It has no continental HSR network. Almost none of its major cities are connected by rail to each 
other. The journey from Cairo to Cape Town — the full length of the continent — cannot be 
made by any existing or planned rail service. 
The Genesis African network is built on a simple and powerful principle. Africa is a peninsula — 
an enormous landmass with long coastlines and population concentrated along those coastlines 
and the great river systems of the interior. The natural network geometry follows the coastlines 
with connecting veins through the middle. 
The East African spine. 
Cairo south through Sudan — through Gaia in the Nubian Corridor, making the world’s largest 
planned city also the world’s most strategically positioned rail hub — continuing south through 
Eritrea to Djibouti, connecting to the existing Ethiopia-Djibouti Railway into Addis Ababa, south 
through the existing Kenya SGR through Nairobi, continuing through Tanzania, Mozambique, 
and Zimbabwe to Johannesburg and Cape Town. 
Total length of the east spine: approximately 8,000km. Much of the southern section exists in 
some form — the Kenya SGR, the Tanzania SGR proposals, South Africa’s existing rail network. 
The northern section from Cairo to Nairobi is the primary construction requirement. Gaia sits 
almost exactly at the midpoint of this spine. Every train travelling the length of Africa’s east coast 
passes through the world’s largest city. 
The West African spine. 
Cairo west along the North African coast through Libya, Tunisia, Algeria, Morocco — connecting 
into the existing Moroccan rail network and approaching the Gibraltar Strait at the northwestern 
tip of Africa. Then south from Morocco down the Atlantic coast through Mauritania, Senegal, 
Guinea, Sierra Leone, Côte d’Ivoire, Ghana, and Nigeria — the most economically significant 
corridor on the continent’s west coast, connecting Lagos, Accra, Abidjan, and Dakar into a 
single continuous network. Continuing south through Cameroon, Gabon, Congo, Angola, and 
Namibia to connect with South Africa’s existing network at the Cape. 
Total length of the west spine: approximately 10,000km. The North African section along the 
Mediterranean coast is the most developed — Morocco, Algeria, and Tunisia all have existing 
rail infrastructure that needs upgrading rather than building from scratch. The West African 
Atlantic coast section is almost entirely new construction. The population density along this 
corridor — Nigeria alone has 220 million people — makes the economic case for this spine 
among the strongest of any rail project on earth. 
The connecting veins. 
Three primary east-west connections thread through the continental interior, linking the two 
coastal spines and connecting the Genesis cities to both. 
The Sahel vein runs east-west approximately along the 15th parallel — from Gaia in the Nubian 
Corridor westward across Chad, Niger, Mali, and into Senegal and the West African Atlantic 
coast. Approximately 5,000km. This is the connection between Gaia and the West African 
Genesis city, making them nodes on the same network. 
The equatorial vein runs east-west through the heart of the continent — Nairobi to Kampala, 
through the Democratic Republic of Congo, into the Republic of Congo and Cameroon, 
connecting to Nigeria and the west coast spine. Approximately 4,000km. This corridor passes 
through or near the Copperbelt city, making it the central node of the continental network’s 
middle section. 
The southern vein connects the existing South African rail network to both coasts and through to 
the continental network to the north — east-west across Botswana, Zimbabwe, and 
Mozambique. 
Total Genesis African network: approximately 25,000-30,000km of new or upgraded rail. 
Comparable in scale to what China built domestically in its first decade of HSR expansion. 
Requiring the same Chinese construction partnership, the same Genesis financing architecture, 
and the same founding charter logic that applies to every Genesis city — with every major node 
in the network running through a Genesis city, which provides the economic anchor and the 
political legitimacy that makes sovereign nations willing to participate in the network rather than 
view it with suspicion. 
 
Section 6: The Indian Network — The Triangle and the Ganges Plain 
India is the world’s most populous nation. Its geography is a triangle — a vast peninsula defined

across its northern width and the Deccan plateau filling its interior. 
India has existing rail — the Indian Railways network, one of the largest in the world by route 
length — but it operates at freight speeds on most lines and has minimal true HSR capacity. The 
Mumbai-Ahmedabad corridor, under construction with Japanese Shinkansen technology, is 
India’s first dedicated HSR line. It is approximately 508km. India needs approximately 
15,000km. 
The Genesis Indian network follows the natural geography of the subcontinent. 
The west coast spine: Mumbai north to Ahmedabad and beyond into Gujarat, south from 
Mumbai through Goa, Mangalore, Kochi, and Thiruvananthapuram to the southern tip of the 
subcontinent at Kanyakumari. Approximately 2,500km along one of the most economically 
active coastlines in South Asia. 
The east coast spine: Kolkata south through Bhubaneswar, Visakhapatnam, Chennai, and 
Madurai to Kanyakumari at the southern tip — where it meets the west coast spine to complete 
the peninsular loop. Approximately 2,200km along a coast that contains some of India’s 
fastest-growing cities. 
The Ganges plain spine: Delhi east through Agra, Lucknow, Varanasi, and Patna to Kolkata. 
Approximately 1,500km across the most densely populated corridor on earth — 400 million 
people living within 100km of this route. The economic return on HSR investment along this 
corridor is among the highest of any rail project in the world. It connects India’s political capital 
to its eastern economic hub through the population heart of the subcontinent. 
The Delhi-Mumbai corridor: Already partially planned, connecting India’s political capital to its 
financial capital. Approximately 1,400km. The completion of this line with the coastal spines and

Delhi north to Lahore and the Pakistan connection: Geopolitically the most sensitive rail

Genesis represents. A rail connection between India and Pakistan that is owned by neither, 
financed by neither, and governs the border crossing through the same transparent multilateral 
framework that governs every Genesis infrastructure crossing, removes the bilateral sovereignty 
questions that have made this connection politically impossible for decades. Genesis does not 
take sides. The train does not care about the partition of 1947. The financial architecture does 
not have a vote in the Kashmir question. The connection serves both populations. The

Total Genesis Indian network: approximately 12,000-15,000km. 
 
Section 7: The Connecting Crossings — Technology Matched to Crossing 
The continental networks described in Sections 5 and 6 are connected to each other and to 
Europe and Asia through a series of water crossings. Each crossing has specific characteristics 
— width, depth, wave conditions, seabed geology — that determine which engineering 
approach is appropriate. 
Three technologies, matched to three categories of crossing: 
Category one: Seabed tunnel. The Channel Tunnel model. Tunnel boring machines drill through 
rock below the seabed. Proven technology. Optimal for crossings where the seabed depth is 
manageable — up to approximately 100m — and the geology is suitable for TBM operation. The 
Channel Tunnel: 50km, 40m depth, 32 years of operation. Japan’s Seikan Tunnel: 54km, 140m 
below seabed, operational since 1988. These are not experimental concepts. They are the most 
used long sea crossings on earth. 
Category two: Submerged floating tunnel. The concept Norway has been developing for 
decades and is now moving toward construction. Two parallel concrete tubes suspended at a 
consistent depth below the surface — typically 20 to 30 metres — held in position by cables to 
the seabed below or pontoons at the surface above. Ships pass freely above. Submarines pass 
freely below. The structure is not affected by surface wave conditions because it sits below 
them. 
The inner tube concept — a protected inner passage within the structural outer tube — is the 
logical refinement that provides complete isolation of the transport corridor from any water 
ingress risk. The outer tube handles structural loads and water pressure. The inner tube carries 
the train in a fully controlled environment. The same principle that the Channel Tunnel applies 
through its central service tunnel, extended to a floating rather than fixed structure. 
This technology is optimal for crossings that are too wide for conventional bridges, too deep for 
seabed tunnels, but too long for a simple immersed tube approach. The Djibouti-Yemen 
crossing at 30km and the Gibraltar crossing at 14km are both candidates. Neither is too deep 
nor too wide for a floating tube structure. Both face more significant sea conditions than 
Norwegian fjords, making the submerged depth of the floating tube — below wave action — a 
structural advantage over a surface approach. 
Category three: Protected surface causeway. For shorter crossings in relatively sheltered 
waters, an elevated surface crossing with breakwater walls on both sides — high enough to 
dissipate wave energy before it reaches the track — is viable and in some conditions preferable. 
The Øresund link between Denmark and Sweden uses this approach partially. The Hong 
Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge uses it across 55km of relatively sheltered Pearl River estuary 
water. The principle scales to crossings of moderate length in conditions where wave heights 
are manageable and construction logistics support surface work. 
The specific crossings and their technology: 
Gibraltar — 14km, 300m depth, Atlantic swell. Submerged floating tunnel. The depth eliminates 
a seabed tunnel option. The width and the existing engineering knowledge base make SFT the 
obvious approach. This crossing would connect the European HSR network directly to the 
Genesis African west coast spine. Europe to Africa in under 30 minutes. The geopolitical and 
economic consequences are significant — Morocco already has a functioning rail network that 
connects at Tangier directly to the crossing point. Spain’s AVE terminates in Algeciras, directly 
opposite. The physical connection is 14km of water. It is the shortest gap between two 
continents on earth. That it has not been built already reflects political hesitation rather than 
engineering impossibility. 
Djibouti to Yemen — 30km, 100-200m depth, Red Sea conditions. Submerged floating tunnel or 
immersed tube. This crossing connects the Genesis African east coast spine to the Arabian 
Peninsula and from there to the existing Saudi Haramain HSR, to Turkey, and through the 
Central Asian corridor to China’s network. It is the single most strategically significant crossing 
for the global Genesis network — the link that connects African rail to Asian rail and through 
Asia to European rail. At 30km the crossing is entirely within the range of proven technology. 
The Bab-el-Mandeb strait, one of the world’s busiest shipping lanes, requires a deep-enough 
submerged structure to avoid conflict with maritime traffic — the floating tunnel’s natural 
operating depth is ideal for this constraint. 
Messina Strait — Italy to Sicily, 3km, 150m depth. The shortest major sea crossing in Europe. 
Has been proposed as a bridge for decades and repeatedly postponed for political rather than 
engineering reasons. At 3km it is trivially buildable by any of the three technologies. A floating 
tube or immersed tube crossing here connects the Italian rail network to Sicily and, through 
planned connections, to Tunisia across the 150km Sicily-Tunisia crossing. 
Sicily to Tunisia — 150km, up to 600m depth. The most technically ambitious of the near-term 
crossings. Too wide for a surface causeway in open Mediterranean conditions. Too deep in 
places for a simple seabed tunnel. The submerged floating tunnel approach — suspended at 
consistent depth below wave action, tethered to the seabed through cables — is the candidate 
technology. At 150km it would be substantially longer than any floating tunnel yet built, requiring 
the engineering development that Norway’s programme is generating and that China’s sea 
crossing experience will contribute to. This crossing connects Italy through Sicily to North Africa 
and into the Genesis African network — creating a second route for European rail to reach 
Africa alongside Gibraltar. 
Bering Strait — Alaska to Russia, 85km, 50m average depth. A seabed tunnel. The depth is 
manageable — significantly shallower than the Seikan Tunnel’s 140m. The geology is basalt — 
stable, suitable for TBM operation. The two Diomede Islands, almost exactly at the midpoint, 
provide natural ventilation shaft locations. The engineering case for this crossing is sound. The 
China Academy of Engineering has formally assessed it as technically feasible using existing 
TBM technology, estimating the tunnel itself at approximately $35 billion over 12-15 years. 
The challenge is not engineering. It is the 5,500km of new railway required on either side — 
through the Alaskan wilderness to connect to the North American network, and through eastern 
Siberia to connect to the Russian Trans-Siberian network. The total project cost exceeds $100 
billion. The geopolitical precondition — a state of diplomatic relationship between the United 
States and Russia that makes a joint infrastructure project viable — is the primary constraint. 
Genesis Monetary Systems changing the architecture of global finance does not directly resolve 
the geopolitical tensions between these specific nations. But a world in which Genesis has 
transformed the relationship between infrastructure investment and sovereign debt — in which 
the financing of large-scale connectivity projects no longer requires nations to choose between 
infrastructure and fiscal sustainability — is a world in which the Bering Strait project’s financial 
case is substantially stronger than it currently appears. When the project’s benefit flows to both 
connected economies without either carrying the debt of building it, the bilateral negotiation 
changes character. 
 
Section 8: The Americas — The Last Major Unconnected Network 
North America has the infrastructure of a continent that chose highways over railways for 
seventy years. The consequences are visible in the data: the United States spends 
approximately $300 billion per year on road maintenance alone. Its freight rail network — 
225,000km of track — is among the world’s most extensive, but it carries freight at freight 
speeds on shared track that cannot support HSR passenger service. 
The Americas case for Genesis investment is the strongest outside Africa — the largest gap 
between economic scale and rail infrastructure of any region on earth. 
North American network: 
The Northeast Corridor from Boston through New York to Washington already carries 
HSR-capable trains and needs infrastructure investment rather than new alignment. The 
California corridor from San Francisco through Los Angeles is under construction. The natural 
extension is the Chicago hub — connecting the Northeast Corridor, the Great Lakes region, and 
the Midwest into a national network, with extensions to the Gulf Coast, the Pacific Northwest, 
and the Canadian corridor from Toronto through Montreal. 
The Mexico City-Guadalajara-Monterrey triangle — 60 million people in Mexico’s three largest 
urban centres — is a natural HSR corridor. Connection northward to the US network and 
southward through Central America toward South America follows naturally. 
South American network: 
Brazil’s São Paulo-Rio de Janeiro corridor — repeatedly proposed and never built — is the most 
economically obvious HSR project in the Americas. São Paulo and Rio together have 
approximately 30 million people. The 500km distance between them is ideal for HSR. 
Connection north to Brasília and beyond, south to Porto Alegre, and west toward Bolivia and the 
Andean corridor follows from the founding spine. 
The Pacific coast corridor — Lima, Quito, Bogotá, connected through the Andean topography 
that makes surface rail challenging but not impossible — provides a western spine that 
complements the Atlantic-facing Brazilian network. 
The gap between North and South America: 
The Darién Gap — the 160km break in the Pan-American Highway between Panama and 
Colombia — is one of the most biodiverse and ecologically sensitive regions on earth. A surface 
rail crossing through the Darién is technically possible and ecologically contentious. A 
submerged crossing through the Pacific coastal shelf — bypassing the most sensitive terrestrial 
environment through an undersea tube — is the approach that preserves ecological value while 
creating continental connectivity. 
 
Section 9: The Dreams — What Comes After 
This document has described what is buildable now, what is buildable within the decade, and 
what requires the decade after that. It closes with what comes after — the crossings that are 
genuinely generational in ambition, that require technology still being developed, that depend on 
a level of international cooperation that the current diplomatic environment does not support but 
that a world transformed by Genesis might. 
These are not plans. They are directions. The direction that civilisation points when it decides 
that connectivity is a human right rather than a commercial product. 
Iceland to Scotland via the Faroe Islands. 
The North Atlantic stepping stones. Mainland Scotland to the Orkney Islands: 20km. Orkney to 
Shetland: 100km. Shetland to the Faroe Islands: 300km. Faroe Islands to Iceland: 450km. Total: 
approximately 870km of sea crossing, broken into manageable segments by the island chain. 
The Faroe-Iceland segment at 450km in open North Atlantic conditions is the engineering 
challenge that no current technology has solved at scale. Wave heights of 8-12 metres in winter 
storms, ocean depths of 200-500m, seismic activity in the Mid-Atlantic Ridge zone. A 
submerged floating tunnel at this length would face wave resonance and current dynamics that 
shorter structures do not encounter. The engineering research required to solve these problems 
is substantial. The solution, when it comes, will be applicable to every long ocean crossing on 
earth. 
Maine to Greenland to Iceland to the United Kingdom. 
The longer northern route. Maine to the southern tip of Greenland: approximately 1,800km of 
open North Atlantic. Greenland to Iceland: approximately 900km. Iceland to Scotland: 
approximately 850km — the Faroe stepping stone route described above. Greenland’s ice sheet 
makes interior rail on the island itself impractical except along coastal routes. The open water 
crossings are the engineering frontier of the century. 
This route, if built, would connect the North American rail network to the European network 
through a northern arc — an alternative to the Bering Strait route that traverses different 
geography and serves different populations. It remains genuinely aspirational. It is stated here 
not as a promise but as a direction — the direction that a world connected by Genesis rail 
eventually faces. 
The transoceanic tube. 
The technology that your intuition points toward — a tube inside a tube, floating below the 
surface at consistent depth, protected from wave action at the top and water pressure by the 
outer structural shell, carrying trains in a sealed inner environment — is the technology that 
makes the longer crossings possible. Not the technology of today. The technology that Norway 
is proving at 4km, that China will scale to 50km, that the century after that will extend to the 
distances that connect continents across open ocean. 
The Channel Tunnel was proposed for the first time in 1802 by a French engineer named Albert 
Mathieu-Favier. It was built 192 years later. The Bering Strait crossing was first proposed by an 
American frontiersman in 1849. It has not yet been built but it will be. The North Atlantic 
crossings described above have no equivalent history of formal proposal — they are genuinely 
new territory. 
But the direction is clear. Every generation of infrastructure engineers has solved the crossing 
that the previous generation declared impossible. The submerged floating tube — sealed, 
protected, carrying trains at HSR speeds below the surface of the world’s oceans — is the 
technology that closes the final gaps. It exists in concept. It will exist in practice. 
 
Section 10: What the Network Produces 
A global rail network of the scale described in this document — the African spines, the Indian 
network, the Asian connecting corridors, the European network, the American network, and the 
water crossings that join them — produces economic and human outcomes that compound at a 
scale difficult to comprehend. 
Every city added to the network gains access to every other city on the network. Not just the 
immediate neighbours. Every node. The economic gravity of network connectivity is not linear — 
it scales with the square of the number of connections, a mathematical principle that explains 
why the internet produced more economic value than any individual component of it and why a 
genuinely global rail network produces more economic value than the sum of its individual lines. 
The specific human outcome is simpler to state. The person in Nairobi who can board a train to 
Cairo, to Lagos, to Johannesburg, to Djibouti, across the strait to Aden, onward to Riyadh and 
Istanbul and Warsaw and Paris — that person’s economic world is not the continent they were 
born on. It is the planet. Their labour, their skills, their ideas, their products, their creativity — 
these reach every market that the network reaches. The network reaches everywhere. 
Genesis cities are the nodes of this network — the points through which the continental spines 
pass, the economic anchors that give the network’s investment case its foundation, the 
governance demonstrations that give the host nations confidence to participate. A world with 
Genesis cities and Genesis rail is not a world where a few well-designed cities exist within the 
current global system. It is a world where the current global system has been supplemented by 
an infrastructure layer that connects the populations the current system was not designed to 
serve. 
The arithmetic of this network, at full development across the century it will take to build, is the 
largest single addition to human economic productivity ever created by a deliberate 
infrastructure investment. Not the largest in some regions. Not the largest in some sectors. The 
largest. Full stop. 
That is what a global rail network produces. 
That is why Genesis builds it.
