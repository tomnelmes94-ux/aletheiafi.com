Section 1: What Money Actually Is 
Before examining what is wrong with the current monetary system it is necessary to examine 
what the current monetary system actually is. Not in the language of economics textbooks. In 
honest terms. 
Money is not wealth. Money is not value. Money is a social technology — a collectively 
maintained agreement that a specific instrument can be exchanged for goods, services, and 
labour. The instrument itself carries no intrinsic value beyond the agreement surrounding it. A 
paper note is paper. A digital currency entry is a number in a database. A gold coin derives its 
monetary value not from the metal but from the collective agreement to treat that metal as a 
medium of exchange. Remove the agreement and the instrument is worth only what it can be 
used for in the absence of the agreement. Paper burns. Gold makes jewellery and conducts 
electricity. The agreement is the money. The instrument is merely its vessel. 
This has always been true. What changes across history is what humanity has been willing to 
agree to treat as that vessel. 
Barter. The original exchange mechanism. One good traded directly for another. No 
intermediary instrument. Functional at small scale within communities where the double 
coincidence of wants — I have what you need and you have what I need simultaneously — can 
be reliably achieved. Unworkable at the scale of complex economies where the division of 
labour produces thousands of specialised goods and services whose exchange cannot be 
coordinated through direct bilateral barter. 
Commodity money. The first monetary technology. Goods with inherent utility and relative 
scarcity — grain, cattle, shells, salt — used as a medium of exchange because their value is 
independently verifiable and broadly accepted. The commodity anchor provides a check on the 
expansion of the money supply — you cannot create more grain by declaration. The constraint 
is also the limitation. The money supply is tied to the supply of the commodity regardless of the 
economy’s productive capacity. 
Metal coinage. The refinement of commodity money. Gold and silver — durable, divisible, 
portable, scarce, and sufficiently rare to resist supply inflation — became the dominant 
monetary commodity across most major civilisations independently. Metal coinage allowed the 
standardisation of exchange, the development of credit, and the expansion of trade across 
distances that commodity money could not practically support. 
Paper money. The first great abstraction. Initially a claim — a receipt for gold or silver held in a 
vault, redeemable on demand. The paper itself was worth nothing. The promise it represented 
was worth the metal backing it. Over time governments discovered that more receipts could be 
issued than the metal held in reserve — that the probability of all holders demanding redemption 
simultaneously was low enough to permit expansion of the paper supply beyond its physical 
backing. This discovery is the origin of fractional reserve banking and of the permanent tension 
between the convenience of paper money and the question of what, precisely, it represents. 
Fiat currency. The final abstraction. Paper — and now digital entries — backed by nothing 
physical. Redeemable for nothing. Worth what the collective agreement says it is worth, 
maintained by government declaration and institutional infrastructure, with no commodity anchor 
of any kind. 
This is what every person on earth uses as money today. It is the vessel of the agreement that 
organises human economic life at global scale. Understanding what it is — precisely, honestly 
— is the prerequisite for evaluating whether it is working and whether something better is 
possible. 
 
Section 2: The Invention of Fiat — And How Money Is Created Today 
The transition to pure fiat currency was not a single event. It was a process that reached its 
completion at a specific and documented moment. 
The Bretton Woods system established in 1944 maintained a partial commodity anchor. The US 
dollar was convertible to gold at a fixed rate of $35 per ounce. All other major currencies were 
pegged to the dollar. Gold remained the ultimate reserve asset — the foundation beneath the 
paper. The system provided monetary stability but constrained the expansion of the money 
supply to the growth of gold reserves, which constrained the fiscal flexibility of governments 
whose spending ambitions exceeded the discipline the gold anchor imposed. 
On 15 August 1971 the President of the United States announced that the dollar would no 
longer be convertible to gold. The Bretton Woods system ended. Every major currency on earth 
became fiat simultaneously. Since that moment no currency in widespread international use has 
been redeemable for any physical asset. Every currency is, in precise technical terms, a 
promise. An unsecured promise from a government to a population — that this instrument will 
be accepted, that its purchasing power will be managed responsibly, and that the agreement will 
be honoured indefinitely. 
Understanding how money is created within this system is essential to evaluating it honestly. 
The process is not complicated. It is simply not widely understood because it is rarely explained 
in plain language. 
How fiat money is created — the actual process: 
A government decides to spend more than it collects in tax revenue. It issues bonds — debt 
instruments — representing its promise to repay the borrowed sum with interest at a future date. 
Those bonds are sold at auction to financial institutions and investors. 
When the central bank participates — as it does extensively through quantitative easing — the 
process works as follows. The central bank creates new money. Not by printing physical notes 
in the first instance. By making a digital entry — increasing the number in its own account. It 
uses that newly created money to purchase government bonds from financial institutions. The 
financial institutions now hold the newly created money. The government has received the 
proceeds of the bond sale and can spend them. The central bank holds the bonds — the 
government’s promise to repay — as an asset on its balance sheet. 
The money that now exists in the financial system did not exist before the central bank created 
it. It was not transferred from somewhere else. It was created by a digital entry in a central bank 
ledger. Backed by a government bond. Which is backed by the government’s promise to repay. 
Which is backed by the government’s ability to tax its population. Which is backed by the 
productive capacity of the economy and the willingness of the population to continue accepting 
the currency as valid. 
At every step the foundation is a promise rather than a physical asset. 
The commercial banking system extends this further through fractional reserve banking. When a 
deposit is made at a commercial bank the bank is required to hold only a fraction of that deposit 
in reserve — typically 3 to 10 percent depending on jurisdiction and regulatory framework. The 
remainder can be lent out. When it is lent out it becomes a deposit at another bank. Which lends 
out the majority of it. Which becomes a deposit elsewhere. Which is lent again. 
The practical consequence is that for every unit of base money created by the central bank the 
commercial banking system creates a multiple of that amount in broad money supply through 
successive rounds of lending. Money is not found. It is not transferred. It is created — by central 
banks through asset purchases and by commercial banks through lending — as a function of 
the system’s design. 
The simplified flow: 
Government needs money → issues bonds → central bank creates new money → buys bonds 
→ new money enters banking system → banks lend multiples of deposits → broad money 
supply expands → more currency chasing same goods and services → purchasing power of 
existing currency dilutes → this dilution is inflation → inflation is a tax on everyone holding the 
currency → it falls most heavily on those with the least ability to hold assets that appreciate with 
inflation. 
This is not a conspiracy. It is the documented mechanical operation of the current monetary 
system. It is how money works. Most people who use money every day — who earn it, save it, 
spend it, worry about it — have never seen this process described in plain language. They have 
been told, implicitly and explicitly, that money is a store of value, a reliable measure of worth, a 
neutral medium of exchange. The mechanism described above is inconsistent with all three of 
those descriptions. 
 
Section 3: The Trust Audit 
The fiat system is a promise. The relevant question for any promise is whether the promisor is 
trustworthy. 
The data on institutional trust is not ambiguous. The Edelman Trust Barometer — an annual 
global survey of institutional trust across 28 countries — has documented a sustained 
multi-decade decline in trust in government institutions across virtually every major democracy. 
In 2024 fewer than half of respondents in most developed economies reported trusting their 
government to do what is right. In several major democracies the figure was below 30 percent. 
This matters for monetary architecture in a direct and practical way. Fiat currency derives its 
value entirely from collective acceptance — from the agreement that the promise is credible and 
will be honoured. When the population making that agreement loses confidence in the promisor 
the agreement weakens. When it loses confidence rapidly the agreement can collapse. This is 
not theoretical. It has happened repeatedly throughout monetary history. 
The Weimar Republic’s hyperinflation of 1921 to 1923 destroyed the value of the German mark 
entirely. At its peak prices doubled every few days. Workers were paid twice daily so they could 
spend their wages before inflation eroded them further. The cause was not an absence of 
money. It was the collapse of confidence in the promise behind the money — the government’s 
willingness and ability to maintain its value — combined with the mechanical creation of 
currency to fund obligations the economy could not sustain. 
Zimbabwe’s hyperinflation of 2007 to 2009 produced a 100 trillion dollar note that could not 
purchase a loaf of bread. Venezuela’s bolivar lost 99.9 percent of its value between 2016 and 
2021. Argentina has experienced multiple currency crises across the past four decades — each 
one a demonstration of what happens when the population’s confidence in the government’s 
monetary promise degrades below the threshold required to maintain the agreement. 
These are not anomalies in a system that otherwise reliably honours its promise. They are the 
documented endpoint of the trajectory that the mechanical processes described in Section 2 
produce when they are pushed beyond the tolerance of the agreement that sustains them. They 
are more frequent in nations with less institutional capacity to manage the process. They are not 
impossible in nations with more. The difference is degree and time horizon, not fundamental 
mechanism. 
The populations living through these events did not make bad decisions. They trusted the 
promise. The promise was broken. Their savings, their wages, their accumulated economic 
security — denominated in a currency that destroyed their value — were transferred to nothing. 
Not redistributed. Destroyed. The purchasing power that represented years of productive human 
effort ceased to exist because the agreement that gave it value ceased to hold. 
This is the risk embedded in every fiat currency system regardless of the sophistication of its 
management. The promise can always fail. It has always eventually failed in every historical 
instance. The question for any given currency is not whether but when — and what the 
conditions are that determine that timeline. 
 
Section 4: The Mechanisms of Management — And What They Reveal 
The current fiat system does not operate without management. It requires continuous active 
intervention to maintain stability. The existence and scale of those interventions is itself 
informative about the system’s underlying condition. A structurally sound foundation does not 
require continuous extraordinary measures to remain functional. 
Quantitative Easing. Between 2008 and 2022 the central banks of the world’s major economies 
— the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan — 
collectively created and deployed over $25 trillion in new money through asset purchase 
programmes. The stated purpose was to prevent financial system collapse following the 2008 
crisis and to stimulate economic activity during the recovery. The mechanism was the one 
described in Section 2 — central bank creates money, purchases assets, money enters the 
financial system. The consequence was that asset prices — stocks, bonds, real estate — 
inflated substantially as the new money sought returns. The owners of those assets — 
disproportionately the wealthiest fraction of the population — saw their net worth increase 
substantially. The holders of wages and cash savings — disproportionately the majority of the 
population — saw their purchasing power erode. Quantitative easing is monetary policy. It is 
also a wealth transfer mechanism. The direction of the transfer is consistent and documentable. 
Fractional Reserve Banking. Commercial banks are permitted to lend multiples of their actual 
deposits. The money created by this lending is real in the sense that it circulates, purchases 
goods, and registers as economic activity. It is also created from a fraction of the actual value 
backing it. In a stress scenario — when a significant proportion of depositors seek to withdraw 
simultaneously — the fraction held in reserve is insufficient to meet demand. This is a bank run. 
It is not a theoretical risk. It is a recurring feature of fractional reserve banking systems 
throughout their history. The response to bank runs is deposit insurance — a government 
guarantee that depositor funds will be protected. Deposit insurance is a contingent liability of 
government. It is another promise backing the promise. The architecture of promises extends 
downward indefinitely without reaching a physical foundation. 
Interest Rate Manipulation. The primary active lever of monetary management. Central banks 
set the rate at which they lend to commercial banks. Commercial banks set their lending rates 
relative to this base. The entire interest rate structure of the economy — mortgage rates, 
business loan rates, savings rates, sovereign borrowing costs — is influenced by this single 
administratively determined number. When rates are low capital is cheap, borrowing is 
incentivised, asset prices rise, and the economy is stimulated. When rates are high capital is 
expensive, borrowing contracts, asset prices fall, and inflation is suppressed. The determination 
of where rates should be at any given moment is a judgement made by appointed officials at 
central banks operating under mandates set by governments. It is neither market-determined 
nor democratically accountable. Its consequences are distributed across the entire population 
— most directly through mortgage costs, savings returns, and employment levels — without any 
mechanism for the affected population to influence the decision. 
Perpetual Debt Rollover. As documented in Papers 2 and 3 the standard operating practice of 
sovereign debt management in most major economies is not repayment of principal but 
continuous refinancing. A bond matures. A new bond is issued to retire it. The principal is never 
reduced. It is transferred forward in time. The interest compounds on the accumulating stock. 
The pretence that the debt will eventually be repaid — that this is a temporary arrangement 
rather than a permanent structural feature — is maintained in political communication while the 
mechanics of the system make repayment structurally implausible. This is not concealment in 
the conventional sense. The information is available. The pretence is maintained because 
acknowledging that sovereign debt is permanent rather than temporary would require 
acknowledging that the promise at the foundation of the system has a specific and 
uncomfortable character. 
Each of these mechanisms is a response to a structural requirement of the system. Quantitative 
easing exists because the financial system periodically cannot sustain itself without central bank 
intervention. Fractional reserve banking exists because full reserve banking would constrain 
credit creation to a degree incompatible with current economic scale. Interest rate manipulation 
exists because the alternative — allowing market forces to set rates without administrative 
intervention — produces volatility the system cannot absorb. Perpetual debt rollover exists 
because the debt cannot be repaid. 
The interventions reveal the condition of the system they are managing. 
 
Section 5: The Accountability Vacuum 
The monetary system described in the preceding sections operates at a scale — and with 
consequences for the lives of billions of people — that would ordinarily demand rigorous 
accountability. That accountability does not exist in a form commensurate with the scale of the 
power being exercised. 
The United States Department of Defense is the world’s single largest institutional budget 
holder. Its annual budget exceeds $800 billion. It has been subjected to formal audit processes. 
It has failed every audit it has been subjected to — not once, not occasionally, but as a 
documented recurring finding across multiple consecutive audit cycles. The most recent audit 
found that the Department could not account for the disposition of approximately $3.8 trillion in 
assets. This is not a rounding error. It is not a record-keeping inefficiency. It is the inability of the 
world’s most powerful government institution to account for trillions of dollars of obligations, 
assets, and expenditures drawn from the tax revenues of its citizens. No individual has been 
held accountable for this failure. No fundamental reform of the systems producing it has been 
implemented. The audits continue to fail. The budget continues to be appropriated. 
The United Kingdom’s monetary management presents a parallel accountability gap in a 
different institutional form. The Bank of England’s decision to implement quantitative easing — 
purchasing gilts through newly created money — added the mark-to-market losses on those 
holdings to the Treasury’s balance sheet when interest rates subsequently rose. The exposure, 
estimated at £100 to £150 billion, represents a liability of every UK taxpayer. It was incurred 
through a process that involved no parliamentary vote, no public consultation, no democratic 
mandate, and no mechanism for the affected population to contest the decision. The Bank of 
England acted within its mandate. Its mandate was set by government. The government that set 
the mandate was not the government that bore the consequences. The taxpayers who bear the 
consequences had no direct input into any stage of the process. 
Central bank independence — the structural insulation of monetary policy decisions from 
democratic accountability — is the standard governance model across the developed world. It is 
defended on the grounds that monetary policy requires technical expertise and long time 
horizons that democratic processes cannot reliably produce. This defence has genuine merit. It 
also means that one of the most consequential levers of economic life — the mechanism that 
determines the purchasing power of wages, the cost of mortgages, the returns on savings, and 
the fiscal position of governments — is operated by appointed officials accountable to no direct 
democratic mandate from the populations most affected by their decisions. 
The combination of these accountability gaps — the inability of governments to account for their 
own expenditures, the insulation of monetary policy from democratic oversight, and the 
accounting frameworks that permit the exclusion of real obligations from official figures — 
constitutes a structural vacuum at the centre of the current monetary architecture. The system 
makes promises to populations. It is not structurally required to account for how those promises 
are managed, whether the management serves the interests of the populations the promises 
were made to, or whether the promises can ultimately be honoured.

Section 6: The Bandages 
The plan that became the Federal Reserve Act was drafted in secret. In November 1910, 
Senator Nelson Aldrich of Rhode Island organised a covert retreat to the Jekyll Island Club off 
the coast of Georgia — described at the time as the most exclusive private club in the world, its 
membership comprising the Morgans, the Rockefellers, and the Vanderbilts. He assembled six 
men: himself, his private secretary, the Assistant Secretary of the United States Treasury, a 
senior partner at J.P. Morgan, the President of National City Bank, and Paul Warburg of Kuhn, 
Loeb & Co. J.P. Morgan, a co-owner of the club, arranged their use of the facilities. To keep the 
meeting secret, each man arrived separately at a train terminal in New Jersey and boarded 
Aldrich’s private rail car one at a time. They used only first names for the duration of the journey 
so that staff could not identify them. One attendee carried a borrowed shotgun to maintain the 
cover story of a duck-hunting trip. They worked for nine days. They denied the meeting had ever 
occurred for twenty years, until the 1930s when competing claims of credit forced their hand. 
The plan drafted on Jekyll Island became, with minor revisions, the Federal Reserve Act passed 
by Congress on December 23, 1913. 
This is not presented here as conspiracy. It is presented as the factual and thoroughly 
documented origin of the institution whose stewardship of the dollar has produced a 96% loss in 
purchasing power over the 112 years since. The Jekyll Island meeting was not a meeting of 
villains. It was a meeting of the most powerful financial actors in the country, designing a system 
to solve a genuine problem — the banking panics that J.P. Morgan himself had been personally 
resolving through private intervention. They solved the banking problem. The system they 
designed to solve it created a monetary one. The men on Jekyll Island represented the apex of 
the financial world that had built its position within the existing monetary architecture. They 
designed what came next from exactly that position. Non-neutral architects cannot produce 
neutral systems. The Federal Reserve is not an exception to that observation. It is its clearest 
historical illustration. 
These papers are being written by a non-profit with nothing to gain. No private rail car. No 
borrowed shotgun. No cover story. Published openly, offered without restriction, and submitted 
to the scrutiny of anyone who chooses to apply it. The contrast with Jekyll Island is not 
rhetorical. It is structural. It is precisely the distinction Paper 1 established as the standard by 
which any proposal for monetary reform should be judged. 
A system in sound structural condition does not require continuous emergency intervention to 
remain functional. The succession of interventions catalogued in this section did not begin in 
2008. It began in 1913. Understanding why requires examining the record that preceded it. 
The United States dollar was established by the Coinage Act of 1792 — a new currency for a 
new nation, defined in silver and gold, with no central bank and no mechanism for a government 
to expand the money supply by declaration. Over the following 121 years, from 1792 to 1913, 
the average annual inflation rate of the US dollar was 0.4% — confirmed by the St. Louis 
Federal Reserve’s own historical analysis — A 62% INCREASE in value. Prices rose in some 
years and fell in others. They rose during the War of 1812. They fell during the long deflation 
that followed. They rose again during industrialisation and fell during the contractions that 
followed overinvestment. The natural motion of a commodity-backed currency responding to 
harvests, production cycles, and trade flows. The price level moved. It moved in both directions. 
But across 121 years, the average annual change in the cost of living was less than half a 
percentage point. 
During those 121 years the United States was not financially calm. Banking panics struck in 
1837, 1873, 1893, and most severely in 1907. Each was presented at the time — and has been 
characterised since — as evidence of a monetary system in need of stabilisation. Examining 
each one reveals a more precise diagnosis. 
The Panic of 1837 was driven by rampant land speculation, overleveraged state-chartered 
banks extending credit far beyond their reserves, and the political removal of the Second Bank 
of the United States without replacing its stabilising function. The currency did not cause it. 
Speculative lending caused it, and when the speculation collapsed the banks that had funded it 
collapsed with it. 
The Panic of 1873 was driven by an extraordinary overinvestment in railroad construction — 
33,000 miles of track laid in five years, financed through bond markets that had extended credit 
to projects whose returns could never justify the capital deployed. When Jay Cooke and 
Company, the nation’s largest railroad financier, could no longer service its obligations, it failed. 
The banks that had lent against railroad bonds failed with it. The currency was a spectator. 
The Panic of 1893 was driven by falling gold reserves, a wave of railroad bankruptcies, and 
bank balance sheets weakened by years of lending against assets that were now defaulting. 
Bank depositors, uncertain whether their banks held sufficient reserves, ran to withdraw 
simultaneously. A structural fragility in how banks were permitted to operate — not a failure of 
the currency itself — produced the crisis. 
The Panic of 1907 began with a failed attempt to corner the stock of the United Copper 
Company. The scheme collapsed. The banks that had financed it faced runs. Confidence 
spread to other trust companies. The crisis was so severe that J.P. Morgan — a private citizen, 
not a government institution — personally convened the city’s bank presidents and organised 
the rescue from his own library on Madison Avenue. The currency was not in question. The 
architecture of private banking, operating without a lender of last resort, was. 
There is one genuine exception in this period. During the Civil War, President Lincoln authorised 
the issuance of unbacked paper currency — the Greenbacks — to fund a conflict whose cost 
could not be met through taxation or conventional borrowing. By 1864, at the war’s lowest point, 
it took $2.58 in greenbacks to purchase one dollar in gold. A genuine debasement, produced by 
a genuine emergency. What followed is the instructive part. Congress limited the total issuance. 
The greenbacks were gradually contracted. By December 1878 they had returned to parity with 
gold. By 1879 they were fully convertible. A currency debased by war was corrected through 
disciplined contraction back to its original value. The system, operating under the discipline of a 
commodity anchor, self-corrected. 
In 1913 the Federal Reserve Act was passed. Its stated purpose was to provide an elastic 
currency and eliminate the banking panics that private actors like J.P. Morgan had been 
resolving through personal intervention. What the panics had actually demonstrated was not 
that the dollar was flawed, but that the banking architecture surrounding it — fractional reserves, 
no lender of last resort, inelastic seasonal credit supply — created periodic crises. The Fed was 
created to solve a banking problem. What it introduced was a monetary one. 
Since 1913 the dollar has lost over 96% of its purchasing power. Not through war alone. Not 
through a single crisis. Through the continuous, compounding operation of a monetary system 
that removed the commodity anchor, granted the authority to expand the money supply by 
institutional decision, and created the mechanism by which every intervention described in this 
section became possible. Under the Federal Reserve’s stewardship, average annual inflation 
has run at 3.5% — more than eight times the 0.4% average of the 121 years that preceded it. 
The currency that had held broadly stable across a young, frequently chaotic, industrialising 
democracy has lost almost all of its value in the 112 years since the institution created to protect 
it took charge. 
The bandages that follow are not a sequence of bad luck. They are the predictable output of the 
architecture the Federal Reserve Act inaugurated. 
The history of the current monetary architecture since 1971 is substantially a history of 
successive interventions deployed to prevent the consequences of the previous intervention 
from producing collapse. The 1971 closure of the gold window prevented the dollar from being 
redeemed for gold it could not deliver. The Plaza Accord of 1985 coordinated currency 
intervention to prevent dollar overvaluation from destroying US export competitiveness. The 
savings and loan crisis of the late 1980s required government bailout of hundreds of failed 
financial institutions. The Long-Term Capital Management collapse of 1998 required Federal 
Reserve-coordinated rescue to prevent cascade failure through interconnected financial 
markets. The dot-com collapse of 2000 to 2002 required sustained interest rate reduction to 
prevent deeper recession. The 2008 financial crisis required the largest peacetime government 
intervention in economic history — bank bailouts, central bank asset purchases, fiscal stimulus, 
and emergency interest rate reductions to near zero across all major economies simultaneously. 
The European sovereign debt crisis of 2010 to 2015 required the European Central Bank to 
commit to purchasing sovereign bonds of distressed member states without limit to prevent the 
dissolution of the euro. The Covid pandemic of 2020 required further extraordinary fiscal and 
monetary expansion — adding tens of trillions in government debt and central bank asset 
purchases globally within months. The inflation surge of 2021 to 2023, partly a consequence of 
that monetary expansion, required the most rapid interest rate increases in forty years — which 
in turn produced the bank failures, the mark-to-market losses on bond portfolios, and the 
refinancing cliff documented in Paper 2. 
Each intervention was a rational response to a genuine crisis. Each crisis was at least partially 
produced by the structural features of the system the previous intervention had maintained. The 
bandages are real. The wounds beneath them are also real. A system requiring this frequency 
and scale of emergency intervention across five decades of operation is not a stable system 
that has encountered bad luck. It is a system whose structural foundations require continuous 
management to prevent the consequences of those foundations from becoming visible. 
 
 
Section 7: Something New Is Not Radical — It Is Inevitable 
Every monetary system in human history has been succeeded by another. Barter gave way to 
commodity money. Commodity money gave way to metal coinage. Metal coinage gave way to 
paper backed by metal. Paper backed by metal gave way to pure fiat. Each transition was, at 
the moment it occurred, described by those who benefited from the existing arrangement as 
dangerous, destabilising, or radical. Each transition occurred regardless — because the existing 
arrangement had reached the limits of what it could sustain, and because something better 
suited to the conditions of the moment existed or was constructed. 
The current fiat system, built on government promises rather than verifiable assets, managed 
through mechanisms that require continuous extraordinary intervention to remain functional, 
governed by institutions operating largely outside democratic accountability, and producing the 
debt trajectory documented in Papers 2 and 3 — is not a permanent feature of reality. It is a 
historically contingent arrangement that has been functional for approximately fifty years and is 
showing the structural signs that have preceded every previous monetary transition. 
The question is not whether the current system will be succeeded. Every previous monetary 
architecture has been succeeded. The question is whether the succession is designed in 
advance — deliberately, transparently, with the interests of the full population it will serve as its 
primary design criterion — or whether it is produced by crisis. By the failure of confidence in the 
existing promise. By the disorderly repricing of obligations that the current architecture has 
spent decades obscuring. 
Designed transitions are possible. The Bretton Woods conference of 1944 was a designed 
transition — flawed in the ways documented in this series, but deliberately constructed by actors 
who could see that the previous arrangement was unsustainable and who chose to design what 
came next rather than wait for collapse. The transition to the euro, whatever its structural 
problems, was a deliberate architectural choice made by sovereign nations acting in concert. 
The conditions for the next transition are assembling. The debt trajectory is unsustainable. The 
hidden obligations are real. The trust in the institutions maintaining the promise is declining. The 
mechanisms required to sustain the system are becoming more extraordinary with each cycle. 
The populations most harmed by the architecture are increasingly aware of the disparity 
between what they have been told about money and what money actually is. 
Something new is not a radical proposition. It is the historically normal trajectory of monetary 
systems reaching the end of their functional lifespan. What would be historically unusual — and 
historically valuable — is designing the transition deliberately, transparently, and in the interests 
of all 8 billion people who will live within whatever comes next.
