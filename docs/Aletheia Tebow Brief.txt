THE PROBLEM
Child exploitation networks operate through encrypted, pseudonymous
channels. Investigators face two hard constraints: content inspection
requires viewing the material, placing serious burdens on investigators;
and encryption defeats conventional pattern-matching tools. Networks
persist for years, actors rotate identities, and prosecution-grade evidence is
difficult to assemble at scale.
WHAT AFI SEES
AFI detects behavioral anomalies in signal streams — without ever
inspecting content. It reads the geometry of how data moves: timing,
sequencing, transfer patterns, peer relationships, and coordination
signatures that distribution networks cannot hide regardless of encryption or
identity rotation.
I
Transfer Pattern Detection: File size ranges, session timing, and sequencing
consistent with distribution — detectable through encryption because the
behavioral signature exists in the structure of the transfer, not the content.
I

even as identities rotate. AFI maps hubs, relay points, and edge actors — building
the network picture investigators need for prosecution.

PROSECUTORIAL VALUE
AFI detections cannot be challenged as fruit of the poisonous tree because
no content is ever inspected. AFI observes only the physics of transmission
— timing, topology, behavioral patterns — the equivalent of a detective
noting that the same vehicle appears at the same location on the same
schedule. Observable fact, not unlawful search. AFI intelligence establishes
probable cause for warrants that authorize content inspection by other tools
— the clean foundation prosecutors build from, not a link that can collapse a
case.
