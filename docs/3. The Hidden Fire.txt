Section 1: The Gap Between Reported and Real 
Paper 2 documented $348 trillion in global debt as reported through official channels — 
sovereign balance sheets, IMF databases, BIS reporting frameworks, national budget 
disclosures. That figure is severe. The trajectory it produces is, as demonstrated, structurally 
unresolvable under current conditions. 
It is not the complete picture. 
Every major economy in the world carries obligations that do not appear in its headline debt 
figures. Some are excluded by accounting convention. Some by deliberate structural design — 
obligations moved into separate legal vehicles specifically to keep them off the sovereign 
balance sheet. Some by political consensus — promises made to citizens that are real in every 
practical sense but are not classified as debt because they have not yet been formally issued as 
financial instruments. 
The gap between what is reported and what is real is not a rounding error. In the cases 
examined in this paper it represents obligations that in several instances exceed the officially 
reported debt figures entirely. The world’s real debt burden is not $348 trillion. The mechanisms 
by which the true figure is obscured are worth examining in detail — not to assign blame, but 
because the obscuring is itself a structural feature of the current architecture with direct 
implications for any honest assessment of the global financial position. 
This paper examines three categories of hidden obligation. The systematic underreporting 
embedded in official accounting frameworks. The specific and documented case of the United 
Kingdom — examined in detail as the most thoroughly auditable example of the gap between 
reported and real. And the question that sits underneath all of it — the unfunded pension and 
social security obligations that represent the largest single category of hidden liability in the 
developed world, and the profound misunderstanding that surrounds them among the citizens 
who have spent their working lives believing they are owed them. 
 
Section 2: What People Believe About Their Pension 
Before examining the numbers it is necessary to examine the belief. 
Ask almost any taxpaying citizen in the United States or the United Kingdom who has spent 
decades paying into their Social Security or National Insurance contributions whether they 
believe they are owed their pension or Social Security payment when they retire. The answer, 
surveyed across demographic groups, income levels, and political affiliations, is overwhelmingly 
yes. Not only do they believe it is owed to them. A significant proportion believe they have been 
paying into a personal account — that their contributions have been held, invested, and will be 
returned to them with the accumulated value of years of saving. 
This belief is not irrational. It is the entirely logical conclusion of the language governments use 
to describe these systems. The word “contributions” implies something given and held. “National 
Insurance” implies a policy with a payout condition. “Paying into your pension” implies a pot 
accumulating on your behalf. The framing is consistent, deliberate, and has been maintained 
across generations of political communication. 
It does not correspond to how these systems actually function. 
There is no pot. There has never been a pot. The National Insurance contributions paid by a 
British worker this week are not held in an account bearing that worker’s name. They are 
transferred immediately to fund the pension payments of people who are retired today. The 
Social Security contributions deducted from an American worker’s paycheck this month do not 
accumulate in a personal account. They fund the Social Security payments of current retirees. 
When today’s workers retire, their payments will be funded by the contributions of whoever is 
working at that future point. 
This is not a pension system in any meaningful financial sense of that term. It is a transfer 
payment mechanism — a structured inter-generational transfer in which current workers fund 
current retirees, operated continuously, dependent on the ratio of workers to retirees remaining 
sufficient to fund the obligations, and backed not by invested assets but by the promise of a 
national government to continue the transfer indefinitely. 
Every functional characteristic of debt is present. A government has made a promise to pay a 
defined benefit at a future date to a defined population. That promise was made in exchange for 
contributions. The government did not invest those contributions in assets that would generate 
the returns required to fund the future payment. The future payment will be funded from future 
revenues — taxation of future workers. The obligation is real, recurring, and growing. The asset 
backing it does not exist. 
The distinction between this and a bond — a formal debt instrument — is one of presentation, 
not of economic substance. A government bond is a promise to pay a defined sum at a future 
date, issued in exchange for capital today, recorded as a liability on the sovereign balance 
sheet. An unfunded pension obligation is a promise to pay a defined benefit at a future date, 
generated by contributions today, recorded as nothing on most sovereign balance sheets. 
The economic substance is identical. The accounting treatment is not. 
This matters for the global debt picture because unfunded pension and social security 
obligations represent the largest single category of sovereign liability that does not appear in 
headline debt figures. When those obligations are included the picture changes substantially. 
In the United States the combined unfunded liability of Social Security and Medicare — the 
present value of promises made to current and future beneficiaries beyond the projected 
revenues available to fund them — is estimated by independent analysts at between $100 
trillion and $175 trillion depending on methodology and discount rate assumptions applied. The 
United States reports $39 trillion in national debt. Its real obligations, by the most conservative 
independent estimate, are more than three times that figure. 
In the United Kingdom the unfunded state pension obligation alone is estimated at £1.7 to £1.9 
trillion by the Treasury’s own Whole of Government Accounts. That figure does not appear in 
the £2.95 trillion headline debt number. It is an additional obligation of comparable scale to more 
than half the reported national debt — owed to millions of people who have spent their working 
lives believing they were saving for their retirement and are in fact the future recipients of a 
transfer payment whose funding depends entirely on the tax base available to future 
governments. 
The gap between what these citizens have been told and what is true is not incidental. It is 
structural. It is the product of political systems that made promises whose cost would be borne 
by governments that did not yet exist, using revenues from workers who had not yet been born, 
without creating the financial architecture that would have made those promises fundable in the 
way they were described. 
 
Section 3: The United Kingdom — A Full Audit 
The United Kingdom is examined here in detail not because it is the worst case globally but 
because it is the most thoroughly documented — and because it illustrates with unusual clarity 
the gap between the official debt figure presented to the public and the real obligations that 
figure conceals. 
The official UK public sector net debt figure as of 2025 stands at approximately £2.9 trillion. This 
is the number reported in budget documents, cited in political discourse, and used in 
international comparisons. It represents approximately 96% of GDP. It is the number most 
British citizens, most journalists, and most commentators use when discussing the UK’s fiscal 
position. 
It is not the complete picture of what the United Kingdom owes. 
The following is a category-by-category audit of the obligations that do not appear in that £2.9 
trillion figure. 
Student Loan Portfolio — £270 to £290 billion. Total student loans issued stand at approximately 
£200 billion in principal with over £90 billion in accumulated interest. The Office for Budget 
Responsibility projects that 75 to 85 percent of this sum will never be repaid — graduates 
earning below repayment thresholds for sufficient years to trigger write-off. The unrecoverable 
portion is functionally government spending deferred through a loan mechanism. The Treasury 
will absorb the write-off. It is public debt by economic substance if not by accounting 
classification. 
Local Government Borrowing — £145 to £150 billion. Local authorities in England borrow 
primarily through the Public Works Loan Board — a government lending facility. The borrowing 
is therefore government lending to government entities whose obligations ultimately rest with 
central government. Several local authorities have reached positions where 15 to 25 percent of 
their entire annual budget is consumed by debt service. Councils including Croydon and 
Spelthorne have effectively become insolvent, requiring central government intervention. The 
liability is local in name. It is national in practice. 
NHS and Health Sector Capital Debt — £55 to £65 billion. Private Finance Initiative hospital 
contracts, NHS trust borrowing, and equipment finance arrangements represent contractual 
obligations that legally bind the Treasury over multi-decade terms. This figure does not include 
the projected future operating deficits of NHS trusts — only the capital obligations currently 
contracted. 
Rail and Transport Liabilities — £55 to £60 billion. Network Rail debt of approximately £55 billion 
has been formally on the Treasury balance sheet since 2014 following its reclassification as a 
public sector body. Residual franchise guarantees and rolling stock financing arrangements add 
further exposure. This is not contingent or estimated. It is contracted and certain. 
Education, Housing and Construction PFI Contracts — £45 to £55 billion. Legacy Private 
Finance Initiative contracts for schools, prisons, housing developments, and Ministry of Defence 
facilities represent long-term payment obligations contracted by government with private 
operators. The assets are often government-used. The payment streams are privately owned. 
The obligations are real regardless of how they are classified. 
Bank of England Quantitative Easing Exposure — £100 to £150 billion. The Bank of England’s 
Asset Purchase Facility accumulated gilts during successive rounds of quantitative easing. As 
interest rates rose the mark-to-market value of those holdings fell substantially. The Treasury 
has indemnified the Bank of England against losses on the APF — meaning the loss is a 
Treasury liability. Estimates of the current exposure range from £100 to £150 billion. It does not 
appear in the headline debt figure. 
Contingent Liabilities and Government Guarantees — £50 to £70 billion. HM Treasury 
guarantees covering Covid-era business loan schemes, Ukraine export credit facilities, energy 
supplier rescue schemes, and similar emergency interventions represent real financial exposure 
that crystallises if the underlying positions deteriorate. Conservative estimates place this 
category at £50 to £70 billion. 
Unfunded Public Sector Pension Obligations — £1.7 to £1.9 trillion. The Treasury’s own Whole 
of Government Accounts documents the present value of pension promises made to NHS 
workers, teachers, civil servants, armed forces personnel, and other public sector employees. 
These are not estimated or speculative. They are calculated by the government’s own actuaries 
using the government’s own assumptions. They represent certain future payments to specific 
populations of people who have been promised them as part of their employment terms. They 
do not appear in the £2.9 trillion headline debt figure.

The real figure is approximately £5.4 to £5.6 trillion — approximately 178 to 187% of GDP. 
The gap between the two — approximately £2.5 trillion — is not the product of aggressive or 
uncharitable assumptions. Several of the largest items in that gap, most notably the unfunded 
public sector pension obligation, are calculated by the Treasury’s own accounting framework 
and published in official documents. They are simply not included in the headline number that is 
reported to the public, discussed in Parliament, and cited in international debt comparisons. 
The United Kingdom’s real fiscal position is not 96% of GDP in debt. It is approaching 180% of 
GDP in real obligations — a figure closer to Japan’s current reported position than to the 
manageable picture the official figure presents. 
 
Section 4: The Global Pattern 
The United Kingdom is not unique. It is the most thoroughly documented example of a pattern 
that exists across virtually every major economy. 
The United States carries $39 trillion in reported national debt and an independently estimated 
$100 to $175 trillion in unfunded Social Security and Medicare liabilities. Adding the most 
conservative independent estimate of unfunded liabilities to the reported figure produces a real 
obligation of over $139 trillion — more than three and a half times the reported national debt. 
China’s reported government debt of $18.7 trillion excludes local government financing vehicle 
debt estimated at $7 to $9 trillion, state-owned enterprise obligations that the government will be 
required to absorb in any stress scenario, and the accumulated liabilities of the property sector 
implosion that is currently being quietly socialised onto the sovereign balance sheet. 
Independent analysts estimate China’s real total debt exposure at $50 to $60 trillion — more 
than three times the reported figure. The IMF’s own documentation footnotes that China’s 
reported figures cover a narrower perimeter of government than IMF staff estimates suggest is 
appropriate. 
Japan’s extraordinary 230% debt-to-GDP ratio is the reported figure. It does not fully capture the 
implicit guarantees embedded in Japan’s financial system, the unfunded components of 
Japanese public pension obligations, or the Bank of Japan’s balance sheet exposure following 
decades of asset purchases. 
The pattern is consistent and structural. Official debt figures are constructed using accounting 
frameworks that exclude categories of real obligation by design — not by oversight. The 
frameworks were created by governments with incentives to present the most favourable 
possible picture of their fiscal positions. The result is a systematic understatement of sovereign 
obligations across the global economy that, when corrected, produces a picture substantially 
more severe than the already severe picture documented in Paper 2. 
 
Section 5: Why The Undercounting Is Structural 
The gap between reported and real sovereign debt is not primarily the product of deliberate 
deception in the conventional sense. It is the product of accounting frameworks that were 
designed, adopted, and maintained by institutions with systematic incentives to exclude the 
most politically uncomfortable categories of obligation. 
Unfunded pension liabilities are excluded from headline debt figures because including them 
would require governments to acknowledge explicitly that they have made promises to tens of 
millions of citizens that are not backed by corresponding assets. The political consequence of 
that acknowledgment — in countries where pension entitlement is among the most deeply held 
social expectations — is severe. The incentive to maintain the exclusion is powerful and 
continuous. 
Off-balance sheet financing vehicles — PFI contracts, special purpose vehicles, 
government-backed enterprise borrowing — are structured specifically to achieve the 
accounting result of funding public activities without the obligations appearing on the sovereign 
balance sheet. This is not incidental to their design. It is frequently the primary motivation for 
their use. The United Kingdom’s Private Finance Initiative was explicitly marketed to successive 
governments as a mechanism for funding public infrastructure without adding to reported 
national debt. The obligations are real. The accounting treatment is chosen. 
The consequence is that the official figures used in public discourse, in political debate, in 
international credit assessments, and in the IMF databases cited throughout these papers 
systematically understate the real fiscal position of virtually every major economy. The $348 
trillion in global debt documented in Paper 2 is the visible portion of a larger obligation. The 
complete figure — incorporating unfunded pension liabilities alone across OECD economies — 
likely exceeds $500 to $600 trillion. 
Against a global GDP of $105 trillion that is a real global debt-to-GDP ratio of approximately 500 
to 600%. 
 
Section 6: The Match and The Fuel 
Papers 2 and 3 together establish the complete picture of the global debt position. Paper 2 
documented the visible debt — $348 trillion, accelerating at 9.1% annually, structurally 
unresolvable under current conditions. This paper has documented what is not visible — the 
unfunded pension obligations, the off-balance sheet liabilities, the gap between what citizens 
have been told and what is true. 
The visible debt is the fuel that has been accumulating for decades. The hidden obligations are 
the additional fuel that has been accumulating alongside it — less visible, less discussed, no 
less real. 
The refinancing cliff documented in Paper 2 — trillions in pandemic-era debt maturing at 
significantly higher rates than when issued — is the near-term ignition risk. It does not require 
any new crisis or external shock to materialise. It is a scheduled series of events already 
calendared. 
But the larger ignition risk is the moment at which the gap between reported and real becomes 
undeniable to the populations it affects. The worker who discovers that the pension they 
believed they were owed is a transfer payment whose funding depends on a future tax base that 
demographic change is steadily eroding. The citizen who learns that their government’s real 
debt position is not 96% of GDP but approaching 180%. The bond market that begins pricing 
the hidden obligations alongside the visible ones. 
Markets do not wait for official acknowledgment. When the gap becomes visible — through 
academic analysis, investigative journalism, political crisis, or the simple arithmetic of pension 
systems whose outflows begin exceeding their inflows — the repricing is not gradual. It is the 
nature of confidence-dependent systems that they absorb accumulating pressure invisibly until 
the threshold is crossed, at which point the adjustment is rapid. 
The current global financial architecture contains both the fuel and the match. The question is 
not whether the gap between reported and real will eventually matter. It is whether the 
architecture will be reformed before that question answers itself. 
 
Section 7: The Transition 
Papers 2 and 3 have together documented the debt that is visible and the debt that is hidden. 
The trajectory of both is the same direction and the combined magnitude is sufficient to 
establish that the current architecture cannot resolve these obligations under its own internal 
logic. 
Paper 4 — The Cost of the Current System — does not continue the debt analysis. It steps back 
to examine what the current system produces beyond its balance sheet. The geopolitical 
instability. The wars with financial architecture beneath them. The structural inequality of 
international financial governance. And the billions of people living below any reasonable 
baseline of human dignity within a global system that has the productive capacity to have 
addressed that condition long ago. 
The debt is the mechanism. The cost is what the mechanism produces.
